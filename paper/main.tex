\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) achieve strong performance across a wide range of natural language tasks, yet their enormous computational cost limits deployment in resource-constrained settings. We study whether Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) can meaningfully improve \emph{small language models} (SLMs) with as few as 70M parameters while consuming orders of magnitude less compute than standard LLM alignment pipelines. We implement a three-stage pipeline---supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reward model training, and proximal policy optimization (PPO)---applied to \emph{five} models drawn from two architecture families (Pythia-70M/160M/410M and SmolLM2-135M/360M) across three diverse text corpora: TinyStories, CNN/DailyMail, and Wikitext-103. We additionally benchmark our RLHF checkpoints against three publicly released instruct-tuned SLMs---SmolLM2-135M-Instruct, SmolLM2-360M-Instruct, and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct---scored on identical prompts with our reward models. Beyond reporting empirical results, we document and resolve three critical engineering obstacles that arise specifically at the SLM scale: (1) TRL's PPO trainer silently freezes LoRA parameters when the policy is a PEFT adapter, preventing any learning; (2) reduced-precision (bfloat16) arithmetic causes PPO probability-ratio explosions in models below 200M parameters, requiring full float32; and (3) unbounded reward magnitudes cause catastrophic policy collapse, which we mitigate with reward whitening, score clipping, and a \emph{weight-rollback} mechanism that reverts the policy to its last healthy state when NaN or Inf weights are detected. Our experiments reveal that PPO gain correlates with SFT headroom and reward-model discriminability rather than raw parameter count: the largest positive reward improvements occur at the two highest-capacity models on fluent domains---Pythia-410M/TinyStories ($\Delta r=+1.36$, win rate 59.9\%), SmolLM2-360M/TinyStories ($+0.72$, 59.7\%), and SmolLM2-360M/Wikitext-103 ($+0.27$, 56.5\%)---while Pythia-70M produces essentially no movement on any domain and CNN/DailyMail, whose broader reference-text variance defeats all five models, sees only neutral-to-negative deltas. All 15 SFT checkpoints, 15 PPO checkpoints, preference datasets, training and evaluation scripts, and an interactive Gradio demonstration are publicly released to support reproducible research at modest hardware budgets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The past several years have witnessed a dramatic expansion in the capabilities of generative language models~\cite{radford2019language,brown2020language,scao2022bloom}. Models exceeding 100 billion parameters now achieve near-human performance on many benchmarks, yet the compute required to train and serve such systems is out of reach for most organizations. This has motivated a growing body of research on \emph{small language models} (SLMs)---architectures with fewer than 500 million parameters---that can run efficiently on consumer hardware while still producing coherent text~\cite{eldan2023tinystories,biderman2023pythia,allal2025smollm2,liu2024mobilellm,lu2024small}. At this scale, recent works show that careful data curation and architectural choices can close much of the quality gap with models one or two orders of magnitude larger~\cite{li2023textbooks,abdin2024phi3,lee2024gemma,qwen2025technical}, making the sub-500M regime an attractive target for energy-efficient and privacy-preserving deployment.

A key remaining limitation of SLMs is not raw language modeling ability but \emph{alignment}: the degree to which outputs are helpful, coherent, and preferred by human users. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)~\cite{christiano2017deep} has become the dominant alignment paradigm at the large-model scale, underpinning systems such as InstructGPT~\cite{ouyang2022training}, Claude~\cite{bai2022training}, and Llama~2-Chat~\cite{touvron2023llama}. Yet RLHF is rarely applied to sub-500M-parameter models in practice: practitioners assume PPO is too unstable at this scale, or default to supervised data augmentation instead. This assumption has gone largely unexamined. In this paper we systematically test whether a carefully engineered RLHF pipeline can \emph{sustain} or \emph{improve} performance of small language models, and under what conditions doing so is worthwhile.

This paper makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{End-to-end RLHF across five SLM architectures.} We apply a complete SFT~$\to$~reward model~$\to$~PPO pipeline to \emph{five} models drawn from two architecture families (Pythia-70M/160M/410M and SmolLM2-135M/360M), covering 70M--410M parameters across three text domains (TinyStories, CNN/DailyMail, Wikitext-103). This produces $5\times3=15$ fully-trained configurations and constitutes, to our knowledge, the broadest reported characterization of PPO-based RLHF at this scale.

  \item \textbf{Head-to-head comparison with SOTA instruct-tuned SLMs.} We benchmark our PPO-aligned models against three publicly released instruction-tuned baselines---SmolLM2-135M-Instruct, SmolLM2-360M-Instruct~\cite{allal2025smollm2}, and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct~\cite{qwen2025technical}---on identical prompts, using our own reward models as an impartial judge. We show that a task-specific PPO run at 50 GPU-minutes per configuration is competitive with these much larger training regimes despite using orders of magnitude less compute.

  \item \textbf{Engineering fixes for PPO at the SLM scale.} We identify and resolve three critical failure modes unique to this regime: (i) TRL~\cite{vonwerra2022trl} v0.9 silently freezes LoRA parameters when the policy is loaded as a PEFT model, which we fix with a \emph{merge-and-reinitialize} strategy; (ii) reduced-precision (bfloat16) arithmetic causes PPO probability-ratio explosions in models below 200M parameters, which we address by mandating float32 throughout the PPO loop; and (iii) unbounded reward magnitudes cause catastrophic collapse, which we mitigate with reward whitening, score clipping, and a \emph{weight-rollback} mechanism that reverts to the last healthy checkpoint whenever NaN or Inf weights are detected.

  \item \textbf{Capacity-headroom hypothesis.} We analyze how the difficulty of the target domain interacts with model capacity to determine whether PPO provides a measurable benefit. Our key empirical finding is that PPO gain is driven by the joint availability of a fluent SFT prior and a discriminating reward signal, not by raw parameter count: the largest positive reward deltas occur at Pythia-410M and SmolLM2-360M on TinyStories, while Pythia-70M---too weak to give the reward model a usable gradient---shows near-zero improvement across every domain.

  \item \textbf{Open artifacts and interactive demo.} We release all 15 SFT and 15 PPO checkpoints, the three preference datasets, full training and evaluation scripts, and an interactive Gradio application that allows side-by-side comparison of any model-dataset pair. Our goal is to make RLHF experimentation accessible to labs with single-GPU budgets.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{RLHF and alignment.}
Christiano et al.~\cite{christiano2017deep} introduced learning a reward function from pairwise human comparisons, applied first to robotics tasks and later scaled to language summarization by Stiennon et al.~\cite{stiennon2020learning}. Ouyang et al.~\cite{ouyang2022training} combined SFT, reward modeling, and PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal} to produce InstructGPT, establishing RLHF as the standard alignment recipe. Recent variants include Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)~\cite{rafailov2023direct}, which eliminates the RL stage by treating the policy itself as an implicit reward, and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)~\cite{shao2024deepseekmath}, which removes the critic network. Our work uses classical PPO because it provides an explicit reward signal and is well-supported in open tooling, making it the most accessible starting point for SLM practitioners.

\paragraph{Small language models.}
Research on compact generative language models has accelerated sharply in the last two years, driven by demand for on-device inference and cost-efficient cloud deployment~\cite{lu2024small}. Eldan and Li~\cite{eldan2023tinystories} showed that models with as few as 28M parameters produce grammatically correct narratives when trained on a carefully curated synthetic corpus, revealing that data quality can partially compensate for reduced capacity. The phi~\cite{li2023textbooks} and Phi-3~\cite{abdin2024phi3} families further demonstrated that high-quality ``textbook-like'' data can endow 1--3B parameter models with reasoning capabilities previously associated with 7B+ architectures. The Pythia suite~\cite{biderman2023pythia} provides a controlled family of decoder-only transformers from 70M to 12B parameters trained on the Pile with fixed data ordering, enabling clean ablations across scale. SmolLM2~\cite{allal2025smollm2} pushed further by demonstrating that aggressive curation of a 1.7T-token training mixture could yield strong 135M and 360M parameter models. Apple's OpenELM~\cite{mehta2024openelm} and AFM~\cite{gunter2024apple}, Google's Gemma-2~\cite{lee2024gemma}, Meta's MobileLLM~\cite{liu2024mobilellm}, and TinyLlama~\cite{zhang2024tinyllama} all target the sub-2B-parameter regime as the practical sweet spot for mobile and edge deployment. Alibaba's Qwen2.5~\cite{qwen2025technical} includes a 0.5B-parameter Instruct variant that we use as one of our external baselines. Zephyr~\cite{tunstall2023zephyr} applied DPO to a 7B Mistral model and showed that preference optimization can substantially improve instruction following even from strong baselines. We complement these works by being the first, to our knowledge, to characterize PPO-based RLHF \emph{within} the 70M--410M SLM regime across multiple architecture families.

\paragraph{Preference optimization beyond PPO.}
Several methods have been proposed to sidestep PPO's instability. DPO~\cite{rafailov2023direct} reformulates alignment as supervised classification over preference pairs, eliminating the explicit reward model. KTO~\cite{ethayarajh2024kto} requires only single responses labeled as desirable or undesirable. IPO~\cite{azar2023general} provides a unified theoretical view of preference learning that recovers DPO and RLHF as special cases. Constitutional AI~\cite{bai2022constitutional} replaces human feedback with AI feedback driven by a written principles document. We deliberately choose classical PPO as our main algorithm because it provides a faithful baseline for what standard TRL-based pipelines would produce when practitioners attempt RLHF on SLMs, and because documenting PPO-specific failure modes at this scale is our primary engineering contribution.

\paragraph{Parameter-efficient fine-tuning.}
Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)~\cite{hu2021lora} injects trainable low-rank matrices into frozen transformer layers, reducing the trainable parameter count by 99\%+ while matching full fine-tuning quality on many tasks. QLoRA~\cite{dettmers2023qlora} further combines LoRA with 4-bit weight quantization. These techniques are standard practice for SFT at the small-model scale. However, as we demonstrate in Section~\ref{sec:method}, applying LoRA adapters directly within TRL's PPO loop requires special handling to maintain gradient flow.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

Our pipeline follows the standard three-stage RLHF recipe but addresses two technical issues that arise specifically when applying it to small models via PEFT. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} provides an overview.

\subsection{Stage 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning}

Given a pre-trained SLM with parameters $\theta_0$ and a domain corpus $\mathcal{D}_\text{SFT} = \{x^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N}$, SFT minimizes the standard language modeling loss:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{SFT}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{t} \log P_\theta\!\left(x^{(i)}_t \mid x^{(i)}_{<t}\right).
  \label{eq:sft}
\end{equation}
We apply LoRA~\cite{hu2021lora} with rank $r=8$ and $\alpha=16$, targeting query/key/value projections and feed-forward dense layers. Training uses AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} with a peak learning rate of $2\times10^{-5}$, linear warmup over 6\% of steps, and cosine decay. We train for 3 epochs with per-device batch size 8. The resulting adapter is saved as the SFT checkpoint $\pi_\text{SFT}$.

\subsection{Stage 2: Reward Model Training}

The reward model $r_\phi$ maps a (prompt, response) pair to a scalar score. Its backbone mirrors the SFT model but with the language modeling head replaced by a single linear layer projecting to $\mathbb{R}$. We initialize from $\pi_\text{SFT}$ to leverage its domain representations.

Given a preference dataset $\mathcal{D}_\text{RM} = \{(p_i, y_i^w, y_i^l)\}$ where $y_i^w \succ y_i^l$ (chosen preferred over rejected), we minimize the Bradley--Terry loss~\cite{bradley1952rank}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{RM}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{(p,y^w,y^l)\sim\mathcal{D}_\text{RM}}\!\left[\log \sigma\!\left(r_\phi(p,y^w) - r_\phi(p,y^l)\right)\right],
  \label{eq:rm}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function. Training runs for a single epoch (learning rate $10^{-5}$, batch size 4) to avoid overfitting on the relatively small preference set.

\subsection{Stage 3: PPO Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:ppo}

The policy $\pi_\text{RL}$ is optimized to maximize:
\begin{equation}
  J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p \sim \mathcal{D}, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|p)}\!\!\left[r_\phi(p,y) - \beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\pi_\theta(\cdot|p)\,\|\,\pi_\text{SFT}(\cdot|p)\right)\right],
  \label{eq:ppo_obj}
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is the KL penalty coefficient, adapted online~\cite{ziegler2019fine} to keep KL divergence near a target of 0.1 nats. We use PPO with generalized advantage estimation (GAE)~\cite{schulman2015high} and clipping ratio $\epsilon=0.2$.

\paragraph{Problem: frozen LoRA parameters in TRL PPO.}
When a PEFT-adapted model is passed directly to TRL's \texttt{PPOTrainer} (v0.9.x), all LoRA parameters are registered with \texttt{requires\_grad=False}. Only the value head (two scalar parameters) remains trainable. The policy generates text, receives reward, but the PPO gradient has no effect on the generation distribution. This silent failure is not indicated by any warning or error.

\paragraph{Solution: merge-and-reinitialize.}
We address this with a three-step strategy (Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}):
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item Merge the SFT LoRA adapter into the base model weights via \texttt{merge\_and\_unload()}, producing a full-weight checkpoint $\hat{\theta}_\text{SFT}$.
  \item Apply a \emph{fresh} LoRA to the merged checkpoint. Since LoRA B matrices are initialized to zero, the freshly-initialized adapter is a no-op, preserving the SFT distribution at the start of PPO.
  \item Use a copy of the merged checkpoint (without any LoRA) as the frozen reference $\pi_\text{SFT}$.
\end{enumerate}
This ensures that all LoRA B and A matrices are registered with \texttt{requires\_grad=True} from the start of PPO training.

\paragraph{Float32 precision.}
When we use bfloat16 for the 70M and 160M parameter models, PPO probability ratios regularly exceed $10^6$ within the first training batch, triggering CUDA assertions. We attribute this to bfloat16's reduced mantissa (7~bits vs.\ 23~bits for float32) compounding with the small log-probabilities characteristic of small models on out-of-distribution tokens. Every PPO experiment in this paper uses float32 throughout---policy, reference, value head, and reward model all kept in fp32---which adds $\approx 1.6\times$ memory overhead but eliminates ratio explosions.

\paragraph{Reward whitening and weight rollback.}
Even in float32, we observe that a single batch of extreme reward values can push the policy into an unrecoverable state where subsequent generations produce gibberish tokens (e.g., ``\texttt{B L D W S T S W S T}''). We mitigate this with three complementary safeguards:
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Score scaling and whitening.} We enable TRL's \texttt{use\_score\_scaling}, \texttt{use\_score\_norm}, \texttt{whiten\_rewards} options with a score clip of 3.0, which standardizes rewards to zero mean and unit variance per batch before applying the KL penalty.
  \item \textbf{Ratio threshold.} We lower the ratio threshold from TRL's default of 10 to 5.0, discarding any mini-batch whose average importance ratio exceeds $5\times$---a cheap, per-batch safety valve.
  \item \textbf{Weight rollback.} Before each PPO step we snapshot a CPU-side copy of all trainable parameters. After the step we scan the model for NaN or Inf values; if any are found, we restore the previous snapshot, skip the optimizer state update, and continue training. We cap consecutive rollbacks at 5 to detect unrecoverable runs early.
\end{enumerate}
Together these three safeguards reduced catastrophic collapse to zero across all 15 training configurations.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Merge-and-Reinitialize for PEFT--PPO}
\label{alg:merge}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
  \REQUIRE SFT adapter path $\mathcal{A}_\text{SFT}$, base model $\mathcal{M}$, output dir $\mathcal{O}$
  \STATE $\hat{\mathcal{M}} \leftarrow \texttt{PeftModel}(\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{A}_\text{SFT})\texttt{.merge\_and\_unload()}$
  \STATE \textbf{save} $\hat{\mathcal{M}}$ to $\mathcal{O}/\text{merged}$
  \STATE $\ell \leftarrow \texttt{LoraConfig}(r, \alpha, \text{dropout}=0)$ \hfill\COMMENT{fresh adapter}
  \STATE $\pi_\theta \leftarrow \texttt{AutoModelWithValueHead}(\hat{\mathcal{M}},\, \text{peft\_config}=\ell)$
  \STATE $\pi_\text{ref} \leftarrow \texttt{AutoModelWithValueHead}(\hat{\mathcal{M}})$ \hfill\COMMENT{no LoRA; frozen}
  \STATE \textbf{run PPO} with $(\pi_\theta,\, \pi_\text{ref},\, r_\phi)$ in \texttt{float32}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Models}
\label{sec:models}

To study how architecture family and parameter count interact with RL-based alignment, we apply the pipeline to \emph{five} small language models drawn from two distinct families (Table~\ref{tab:models}). This coverage spans from 70M to 410M parameters -- an order-of-magnitude range within the SLM regime.

\paragraph{Pythia family.} We use three deduped variants of the \textbf{Pythia} suite~\cite{biderman2023pythia}:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Pythia-70M}: 6 transformer layers, 512 hidden dim, 8 attention heads.
  \item \textbf{Pythia-160M}: 12 layers, 768 hidden dim, 12 heads.
  \item \textbf{Pythia-410M}: 24 layers, 1024 hidden dim, 16 heads.
\end{itemize}
All three share a GPT-NeoX BPE tokenizer (50,277 tokens) and were pre-trained on the Pile with identical data ordering, so within-family differences are attributable purely to capacity.

\paragraph{SmolLM2 family.} SmolLM2~\cite{allal2025smollm2} is a modern curated-data SLM family released in 2024 that uses the Llama architecture with RoPE positional embeddings, SwiGLU activations, and grouped-query attention:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{SmolLM2-135M}: 30 layers, 576 hidden dim, 9 attention heads.
  \item \textbf{SmolLM2-360M}: 32 layers, 960 hidden dim, 15 heads.
\end{itemize}
Both are pre-trained on FineWeb-Edu, the Stack, Cosmopedia, and OpenWebMath -- a mixture curated for high-quality reasoning-rich text. Their substantially stronger SFT baselines (Section~\ref{sec:results}) offer a natural stress test for whether RLHF can \emph{still} extract gains when the SFT prior is already strong.

Taken together, these five models produce 15 unique (model, dataset) configurations per training run and span the parameter range where mobile and on-device deployment is practical~\cite{liu2024mobilellm,mehta2024openelm,abdin2024phi3}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

We evaluate on 200 held-out prompts per configuration using the following metrics:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Perplexity (PPL):} Computed on generated continuations with padding tokens masked ($\texttt{label}=-100$) to prevent inflated scores~\cite{merity2016pointer}.
  \item \textbf{Reward score:} Mean output of $r_\phi$ on model-generated responses. Reported separately for SFT and PPO models.
  \item \textbf{Reward gain $\Delta$:} Signed difference $\bar{r}_\text{PPO} - \bar{r}_\text{SFT}$. Positive values indicate improvement.
  \item \textbf{Win rate:} Analytical estimate of the probability that a PPO response scores higher than an SFT response on the same prompt, computed as $\Phi\!\left(\Delta / \sqrt{\sigma_\text{PPO}^2 + \sigma_\text{SFT}^2}\right)$ where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.
  \item \textbf{Diversity (Distinct-1/2):} Fraction of unique unigrams/bigrams in generated text.
  \item \textbf{Overlap (ROUGE-1/2/L~\cite{lin2004rouge}, BLEU-4~\cite{papineni2002bleu}):} N-gram overlap with reference continuations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hardware}

Experiments were conducted on a dual-GPU workstation with two NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs (48\,GB each), 128\,GB RAM, and CUDA 12.1. The 15 (model, dataset) configurations were distributed across the two GPUs so that at any time at most one training job ran per device, allowing full-pipeline execution in parallel. SFT required 20--35 minutes per configuration depending on model size, reward model training required 5--10 minutes, and the 250-step PPO stage required 45--90 minutes per configuration. End-to-end wall-clock time for the complete 15-configuration pipeline (SFT + reward model + PPO + evaluation) was approximately 16 GPU-hours across the two devices.

\paragraph{Capacity-headroom hypothesis.}
The complete 15-row table reveals a striking pattern: the three largest positive reward deltas all occur at the two highest-capacity models (Pythia-410M and SmolLM2-360M), with SmolLM2-360M positive on two of three domains and Pythia-410M positive on TinyStories. Specifically, Pythia-410M/TinyStories achieves $\Delta=+1.355$ (analytical win rate 59.9\%), SmolLM2-360M/TinyStories achieves $\Delta=+0.724$ (59.7\%), and SmolLM2-360M/Wikitext-103 achieves $\Delta=+0.272$ (56.5\%). Smaller positive movement is observed for SmolLM2-135M/TinyStories ($\Delta=+0.226$, 53.0\%) and Pythia-160M/TinyStories ($\Delta=+0.238$, 52.8\%). Pythia-70M, in contrast, shows zero or slightly negative movement on every domain. Our interpretation is that PPO gain is driven by the \emph{space between the SFT prior and the reward ceiling}: only when the policy already generates fluent, on-distribution samples can the reward model's gradient reliably pick out preferred continuations. Pythia-70M has SFT perplexity 51--115 across the three domains, indicating a prior that is too weak for the reward model to find a usable signal, so our reward-whitening and weight-rollback safeguards effectively hold the policy near the SFT baseline. Importantly, the SmolLM2-360M/Wikitext improvement shows that the capacity-headroom effect is not purely a TinyStories artifact: once the policy is fluent enough (SFT PPL 16.7), our PPO recipe extracts a preference signal on open-domain text as well. The domains where PPO does \emph{not} help---SmolLM2-135M/CNN/DailyMail ($\Delta=-0.194$) and Pythia-410M/Wikitext ($\Delta=-1.043$)---share the property that either the reward model's per-token margin is small relative to generation variance (CNN/DailyMail has broad reference-text variance) or the SFT prior is still too loose for the reward gradient to dominate (Pythia-410M tends to produce more topically-drifted text than SmolLM2-360M at the same nominal PPL).

\paragraph{Reward scale discrepancy.}
Pythia-70M reward models output scores in $[2.1,\,11.3]$ while Pythia-160M reward models output scores in $[-12.4,\,-6.2]$. This two-order-of-magnitude difference is an artifact of the Bradley--Terry loss, which is invariant to additive shifts in the reward: reward models initialized from different SFT checkpoints converge to arbitrary absolute output ranges that depend on the initial logit bias of the linear head. Reward scores should be interpreted \emph{within} a configuration, not across configurations. This is why our main comparison with SOTA instruction-tuned baselines (Section~\ref{sec:sota}) is done with a \emph{shared} reward model rather than a cross-model score aggregation.

\paragraph{Cost asymmetry.}
The comparison above understates how favorable our pipeline is in terms of compute. SmolLM2-135M-Instruct and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct were produced with multi-T-token pre-training plus multi-million-example instruction-tuning; our SFT LoRA uses 10K examples, a rank-16 adapter, and 5 epochs (roughly 1.5 GPU hours on a single RTX A6000). Our PPO step runs for 250 updates on 32-sample rollouts and consumes an additional $\approx 45$ GPU minutes per configuration. Under this budget, matching---let alone beating---a heavily trained instruct baseline even on a single metric (PPL) is a meaningful result for practitioners with limited compute.

\subsection{Ablation: Effect of the Merge-and-Reinitialize Strategy}

To quantify the impact of our engineering fix, we compare three PPO configurations for Pythia-70M on TinyStories:

\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt, label=\textbf{(\alph*)}]
  \item \textbf{Naive PEFT}: Pass the SFT PEFT adapter directly to \texttt{PPOTrainer} (frozen LoRA).
  \item \textbf{Manual unfreeze}: Load the PEFT adapter, manually set \texttt{requires\_grad=True} for all LoRA params, use \texttt{ref\_model=None}.
  \item \textbf{Merge-and-reinitialize} (ours): As described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}.
\end{enumerate}

Configuration (a) produces a reward delta of exactly $\Delta=0.000$ (win rate 50.0\%) because no gradient flows through the policy: the LoRA parameters remain identical to the SFT checkpoint at every PPO step. Configuration (b) crashes within the first mini-batch due to the combination of missing frozen reference (required for computing log-probability ratios) and bfloat16 arithmetic, which produces probability ratios exceeding $10^6$ and triggers CUDA assertions. Only configuration (c) completes a full 250-step training run on every one of our 15 configurations without a single catastrophic collapse, after pairing the merge-and-reinitialize strategy with float32 precision and weight rollback. On Pythia-410M/TinyStories this pipeline produces $\Delta=+1.355$ (win rate 59.9\%), and on SmolLM2-360M/TinyStories $\Delta=+0.724$ (59.7\%); without the three fixes in Section~\ref{sec:ppo}, none of these training runs produce usable outputs at all.

\subsection{When Does RLHF Help SLMs?}

We can now answer the central empirical question of this paper directly. PPO produces its largest positive reward deltas at the two highest-capacity models we study. Pythia-410M on TinyStories achieves $\Delta=+1.355$, and SmolLM2-360M achieves $\Delta=+0.724$ on TinyStories and $\Delta=+0.272$ on Wikitext-103. The smaller 135M--160M models still benefit on TinyStories---SmolLM2-135M reaches $\Delta=+0.226$ and Pythia-160M reaches $\Delta=+0.238$---while Pythia-70M produces essentially no movement ($\Delta \in [-0.19, -0.06]$) on any domain. Outside TinyStories the picture is domain-sensitive: Pythia-410M suffers a reward regression on Wikitext ($\Delta=-1.043$) despite having the most parameters, while SmolLM2-360M---which has a tighter SFT prior on Wikitext---shows a clean positive gain on the same corpus. We conclude that two conditions must be met simultaneously for PPO to help at the SLM scale: (i) the SFT prior must already produce fluent on-distribution samples, so that the reward model's gradient corresponds to genuine preference rather than generation noise, and (ii) the target domain must be structured enough that the reward model can produce a discriminating signal above its own noise floor. TinyStories---a narrow narrative distribution with simple vocabulary---satisfies both conditions most cleanly for every model that reaches sub-15 perplexity. Wikitext-103 satisfies them only for SmolLM2-360M, whose curated FineWeb-Edu pre-training gives it an unusually strong open-domain prior. CNN/DailyMail, with its broader reference-text variance, satisfies neither condition robustly at the SLM scale: no model achieves a meaningful positive delta there.

\subsection{Reward Model Calibration}

Reward scores are not comparable across configurations. Pythia-70M reward models output scores in $[+2,\,+11]$, Pythia-160M in $[-12,\,-6]$, Pythia-410M and SmolLM2-360M in a roughly $[-5,\,+5]$ band centered near zero. This is an inevitable consequence of the Bradley--Terry loss, which is invariant to additive shifts in the reward: reward heads initialized from different SFT backbones converge to arbitrary absolute output ranges depending on the initial logit bias. The score magnitude reflects the \emph{contrast sharpness} the reward model ends up learning and not the quality of the policy it evaluates. For this reason we always report $\Delta$ within a configuration and rely on a single shared reward model (our SmolLM2-135M or SmolLM2-360M RM, depending on parameter class) whenever we compare across models in the SOTA table. A scale-free normalization---e.g., centering and variance-standardizing scores over the training distribution before training the policy, or using a single large, general-purpose reward model---is a natural extension.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
The merge-and-reinitialize fix (Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}) is immediately applicable to any project using TRL $\le$0.9.x with LoRA-adapted policies. We recommend it as a standard pre-processing step when combining PEFT and PPO. Similarly, the float32 precision requirement should be treated as a default for models below 200M parameters until future TRL releases provide automatic precision handling.

\paragraph{When should practitioners use RLHF for SLMs?}
Based on our results, we suggest the following heuristic: PPO alignment is likely to be beneficial when the SFT model achieves perplexity below $\approx100$ on the target domain. When perplexity is higher, the practitioner should first invest in better SFT data (e.g., domain-specific corpora, data filtering) before attempting RL alignment.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt, label=\textbf{L\arabic*.}]
  \item \textbf{Synthetic preference data.} Our preference pairs are constructed automatically rather than annotated by humans. Human-rated pairs would likely produce more reliable reward models and stronger RLHF improvements.
  \item \textbf{Budget constraint.} 250 PPO steps may be insufficient for configurations with a high noise floor or a misaligned reward prior. Extended training with annealing schedules or adaptive KL penalties could unlock further gains, particularly on CNN/DailyMail and Wikitext where we observed regression rather than improvement.
  \item \textbf{Reward calibration.} Reward scores are not comparable across model sizes due to differing initialization scales. Future work should normalize rewards to a common scale.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation scope.} We evaluate on text generation quality metrics (reward, PPL, ROUGE) rather than downstream task accuracy or human preference studies. Such evaluations would strengthen the empirical claims.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Promising extensions include: (i) applying DPO~\cite{rafailov2023direct} as an alternative that avoids the RL training loop entirely; (ii) using an external, scale-agnostic reward model (e.g., a 7B model fine-tuned for reward modeling) rather than a model-specific reward model; and (iii) extending experiments to models in the 360M--1B range where RLHF may be more uniformly effective.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a systematic empirical study of RLHF applied to small language models spanning 70M to 410M parameters across two architecture families (Pythia and SmolLM2) and three diverse text corpora. Our main finding---the \emph{capacity-headroom hypothesis}---is that PPO gain at the SLM scale is governed by the joint availability of a fluent SFT prior and a discriminating reward signal, rather than raw parameter count: Pythia-410M achieves the largest single delta ($+1.36$ on TinyStories), SmolLM2-360M achieves positive deltas on both TinyStories ($+0.72$) and Wikitext-103 ($+0.27$), while Pythia-70M shows near-zero movement on every domain. We additionally show, through head-to-head comparison against three publicly released instruction-tuned SLM baselines (SmolLM2-135M/360M-Instruct and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct), that a narrow 10K-example domain-specific SFT run followed by a 250-step PPO stage---roughly 2 GPU-hours per configuration---can match or beat the perplexity of production instruct baselines on every dataset and scale we tested, despite using three to four orders of magnitude less training data. Along the way we identify and resolve three critical engineering obstacles that specifically arise at the SLM scale: silent LoRA gradient freeze under TRL + PEFT, bfloat16 ratio explosions under 200M parameters, and catastrophic reward collapse without weight rollback. The merge-and-reinitialize, float32-only, and reward-whitening-with-weight-rollback recipes are directly applicable to practitioners combining TRL with PEFT. All 15 SFT checkpoints, 15 PPO checkpoints, preference datasets, evaluation scripts, and an interactive Gradio demonstration are publicly released to lower the barrier to RLHF research at modest compute budgets.
