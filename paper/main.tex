% ─── Abstract ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is now the standard recipe for aligning large language models, yet it is almost never applied to the 70--500M-parameter regime where on-device and edge deployment actually live. The prevailing assumption is that Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is too unstable below the billion-parameter scale, and that practitioners at this size should rely on supervised fine-tuning (SFT) alone. We test this assumption directly. We build an end-to-end SFT~$\to$~reward~model~$\to$~PPO pipeline that we apply, without any per-configuration tuning, to \emph{five} small language models spanning two architecture families---Pythia-70M/160M/410M and SmolLM2-135M/360M---and three stylistically distinct corpora: TinyStories, CNN/DailyMail, and Wikitext-103. The resulting $5\times3=15$ fully trained configurations constitute, to our knowledge, the broadest published characterisation of PPO-based RLHF at this scale. Beyond the measurements themselves, we document and resolve three engineering pathologies that are specific to this regime and that silently derail standard TRL-based pipelines: (i) TRL v0.9.x registers every LoRA parameter with \texttt{requires\_grad=False} when a PEFT-wrapped policy is passed to \texttt{PPOTrainer}, so the policy generates and receives reward without ever being updated; (ii) bfloat16 probability ratios explode past $10^6$ within the first mini-batch on sub-200M-parameter models, triggering CUDA assertions; and (iii) unbounded reward magnitudes push the policy into token-salad collapse. We address them with a merge-and-reinitialise strategy for the LoRA adapter, a float32-only precision discipline, and a three-layer safety net that combines reward whitening, an importance-ratio threshold, and a weight-rollback mechanism that snapshots trainable parameters before every update and restores them whenever NaN/Inf values appear. The recipe completes all 15 training runs without a single catastrophic collapse. Empirically, PPO gain tracks the joint availability of a fluent SFT prior and a discriminating reward signal, not raw parameter count: the largest positive improvements occur at the two highest-capacity models on fluent domains---Pythia-410M/TinyStories ($\Delta r=+1.36$, analytical win rate 59.9\%), SmolLM2-360M/TinyStories ($+0.72$, 59.7\%), and SmolLM2-360M/Wikitext-103 ($+0.27$, 56.5\%)---while Pythia-70M, whose SFT perplexity never falls below 50, shows near-zero movement on every domain. Head-to-head against SmolLM2-135M/360M-Instruct and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct, our domain-specific 10K-example SFT reaches lower perplexity on every (model, dataset) pair, and PPO further lifts reward at the 360M scale to a level above both released instruct baselines. We cross-verified every reported metric against the raw per-run evaluation outputs (339 scalar fields in total) before release. All 15 SFT checkpoints, 15 PPO checkpoints, preference datasets, training and evaluation scripts, and an interactive verification app are publicly released on Hugging Face and GitHub.
\end{abstract}

% ─── 1. Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Generative language models have become conspicuously useful \emph{and} conspicuously expensive. Systems with more than a hundred billion parameters now routinely match human performance on broad evaluation suites~\cite{radford2019language,brown2020language,scao2022bloom}, but training and serving them require computational budgets that are out of reach for most academic labs and for essentially every privacy-sensitive or edge-deployed application. This cost asymmetry has driven a recent wave of interest in the other end of the scale: architectures with fewer than five hundred million parameters---\emph{small language models} (SLMs)---that run efficiently on consumer GPUs, mobile SoCs, and embedded devices~\cite{eldan2023tinystories,biderman2023pythia,allal2025smollm2,liu2024mobilellm,lu2024small}. The SLM literature has shown, repeatedly and from multiple directions, that careful data curation and architectural refinement can close a surprising fraction of the quality gap with models one or two orders of magnitude larger~\cite{li2023textbooks,abdin2024phi3,lee2024gemma,qwen2025technical}. The sub-500M regime is therefore attractive as the practical sweet spot for on-device inference and for any organisation that cannot afford to operate a thirteen-billion-parameter server farm.

What this literature has \emph{not} yet settled is the question of alignment. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)~\cite{christiano2017deep,ouyang2022training} is the dominant technique for turning a raw pre-trained model into one that produces helpful, coherent, and preferred outputs; it underpins InstructGPT~\cite{ouyang2022training}, Claude~\cite{bai2022training}, and Llama~2-Chat~\cite{touvron2023llama}. Yet when one actually surveys the open-source SLM literature, essentially no model below the five-hundred-million-parameter mark is aligned with Proximal Policy Optimization~\cite{schulman2017proximal}. The practical folk wisdom, repeated in TRL issue threads and technical reports alike, is that PPO is too unstable at this scale: gradients explode, rewards collapse, and whatever is left after training is usually worse than the SFT starting point. Practitioners default to supervised data augmentation, occasionally to Direct Preference Optimization~\cite{rafailov2023direct}, and rarely interrogate the underlying assumption. In this paper we test that assumption head-on by asking the simplest possible version of the question: \emph{when, if ever, does classical PPO help a small language model, and what does it take to make the training run survive?}

We address the question empirically, at scale, with an end-to-end pipeline that we deliberately leave untuned across configurations. We train five base models---Pythia-70M, Pythia-160M, Pythia-410M, SmolLM2-135M, and SmolLM2-360M---on three stylistically distinct corpora (TinyStories, CNN/DailyMail, Wikitext-103) through a full SFT~$\to$~reward-model~$\to$~PPO cycle, producing fifteen fully trained configurations. We hold every hyperparameter fixed; the only thing that varies is the (model, dataset) pair. This is unusual in the RLHF literature, where results are typically reported for a single model and a single preference corpus, and it exposes how the outcome of PPO depends on the interaction between model capacity and domain difficulty. It also exposes which failure modes are artefacts of a specific configuration and which are structural---a distinction that matters a great deal when one is trying to derive general recommendations for SLM practitioners.

Pursuing this question surfaced three engineering pathologies that are invisible in the published RLHF literature because the published literature almost never operates below the billion-parameter scale. First, when a PEFT-wrapped model is passed directly to TRL's \texttt{PPOTrainer} (v0.9.x)~\cite{vonwerra2022trl}, every LoRA parameter is silently registered with \texttt{requires\_grad=False}: only the two scalar parameters of the value head remain trainable, so PPO rolls out generations, receives reward, and dutifully computes losses, yet the policy distribution never moves. Nothing in the training logs reveals this; the reward trace is flat, a warning one easily attributes to ``PPO is just noisy at this scale.'' Second, bfloat16 arithmetic---normally harmless at the billion-parameter scale because the Boltzmann tail of typical probability ratios does not reach the limit of the format---produces ratio overflows past $10^6$ in the very first mini-batch on models under roughly two hundred million parameters, triggering CUDA assertions and ending the run. Third, unbounded reward values compound with PPO's un-clipped KL penalty to shove the policy into an unrecoverable token-salad regime after a single unlucky batch. Each pathology is individually benign when it occurs in isolation, but together they explain why a direct transcription of the InstructGPT training recipe onto a 70M--400M-parameter model reliably fails. We describe and resolve all three, and our fixes form the core engineering contribution of this paper.

On top of these fixes, the experiments themselves yield a clean empirical story. PPO produces its largest positive reward gains at the two largest models on the two domains where the SFT prior is already fluent: Pythia-410M on TinyStories ($\Delta r = +1.36$, analytical win rate 59.9\%) and SmolLM2-360M on TinyStories ($+0.72$, 59.7\%) and Wikitext-103 ($+0.27$, 56.5\%). The smaller Pythia-160M and SmolLM2-135M show modest but still positive movement on TinyStories, while Pythia-70M---whose SFT perplexity never drops below 50 on any domain---produces essentially no change under PPO, positive or negative. We formalise this pattern as the \emph{capacity-headroom hypothesis}: at the SLM scale, the benefit of PPO is governed not by raw parameter count but by the joint availability of a fluent SFT prior and a discriminating reward signal. The pattern holds across both architecture families and is clearly visible when we place all fifteen $(\text{PPL}_{\text{SFT}}, \Delta r)$ points on a log-scale plot (Fig.~\ref{fig:capacity-headroom}).

We then benchmark the 360M-class portion of our pipeline against three publicly released, much-more-expensively-trained instruct baselines---SmolLM2-135M-Instruct, SmolLM2-360M-Instruct~\cite{allal2025smollm2}, and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct~\cite{qwen2025technical}---and find that a 10K-example, 5-epoch LoRA SFT followed by a 250-step PPO run already produces lower perplexity than all three on every dataset we test, and higher reward at the 360M parameter class on TinyStories and Wikitext-103. Our pipeline consumes roughly $1.5$ GPU-hours per (model, dataset) configuration versus the multi-trillion-token pre-training plus multi-million-example instruction-tuning budget of the released baselines. The asymmetry is the point: for a practitioner with one or two consumer GPUs, a small, well-disciplined RLHF run on a targeted corpus is now a viable alternative to waiting for the next generation of open instruct models.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
This paper makes five contributions.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{The broadest reported PPO-based RLHF sweep at the SLM scale.} We train and evaluate fifteen full SFT~$\to$~reward~$\to$~PPO configurations spanning two architecture families, five model sizes from 70M to 410M parameters, and three stylistically distinct corpora, under a single fixed hyperparameter budget and with zero per-configuration tuning.

  \item \textbf{A head-to-head comparison with released instruct baselines.} At the 360M parameter class, our 10K-example SFT already beats the published perplexity of SmolLM2-360M-Instruct and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct on every dataset, and our PPO stage further lifts reward on TinyStories and Wikitext-103, using three to four orders of magnitude less training data than the released baselines.

  \item \textbf{Engineering fixes for PPO at the SLM scale.} We identify and resolve the three pathologies described above: (i) the silent LoRA-freeze in TRL~\cite{vonwerra2022trl}, which we address with a \emph{merge-and-reinitialise} strategy for the PEFT policy; (ii) bfloat16 ratio explosions, which we address by mandating float32 throughout the PPO loop; and (iii) catastrophic reward collapse, which we address with reward whitening, an importance-ratio threshold, and a \emph{weight-rollback} mechanism that snapshots trainable parameters before every optimiser step and restores them whenever NaN or Inf values appear. Together these safeguards drive catastrophic collapse to zero across all fifteen runs.

  \item \textbf{The capacity-headroom hypothesis.} We show, empirically and over fifteen points, that PPO gain at the SLM scale is determined by the joint availability of a fluent SFT prior and a discriminating reward model---not by raw parameter count---and we quantify the precondition as an SFT-perplexity threshold (approximately $\text{PPL}_{\text{SFT}}<20$) below which PPO reliably helps.

  \item \textbf{Open artefacts and a reviewer-facing verification app.} We release all 15 SFT and 15 PPO checkpoints, the three preference datasets, full training and evaluation scripts, the seven figures in this paper with their generator, and a four-tab interactive Gradio app that lets any reader pick a (model, dataset, prompt) triple and verify SFT and PPO outputs, reward scores, and the corresponding published metrics. Before release we cross-checked every numerical field in the aggregated results file against the raw per-run evaluation JSONs; all 339 fields match.
\end{enumerate}

% ─── 2. Related Work ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{From human preferences to RLHF.}
The modern RLHF pipeline traces back to Christiano et al.~\cite{christiano2017deep}, who showed that a scalar reward function could be learned from pairwise human comparisons and then optimised with policy gradient methods to solve robotics and Atari tasks that would otherwise require hand-crafted reward shaping. Stiennon et al.~\cite{stiennon2020learning} ported the recipe to text summarisation, and Ouyang et al.~\cite{ouyang2022training} combined SFT, reward modelling, and PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal} into InstructGPT, which became the reference architecture for every subsequent aligned LLM. Bai et al.~\cite{bai2022training,bai2022constitutional} and Touvron et al.~\cite{touvron2023llama} scaled the same pipeline to Claude and Llama~2-Chat. All of these works operate on models in the 1.3B--175B-parameter range, and much of the engineering headroom they rely on---large batch sizes, high-bandwidth multi-node interconnect, and near-continuous supervised signal from annotated preference pairs---is not available at the SLM scale. The failure modes we describe in Section~\ref{sec:ppo} are invisible in that regime not because they do not exist, but because the computational envelope absorbs them.

\paragraph{Alternatives to PPO.}
PPO's reputation for instability motivated an entire sub-literature on alignment methods that avoid the RL loop altogether. Direct Preference Optimization~\cite{rafailov2023direct} reformulates preference learning as a contrastive log-likelihood objective, derivable in closed form from the same Bradley--Terry assumption that underlies classical reward modelling, and sidesteps both the reward model and the policy gradient stage. Kahneman--Tversky Optimization~\cite{ethayarajh2024kto} generalises further by accepting single responses labelled desirable or undesirable, rather than paired comparisons. Azar et al.~\cite{azar2023general} give a unified view of preference learning under which DPO, IPO, and RLHF all appear as choices of a single loss functional. Group Relative Policy Optimization~\cite{shao2024deepseekmath} removes PPO's critic network by estimating advantages from the standard deviation of multi-sample rewards within a group. These methods are principled and often more stable, and they are now the default at many labs. We nonetheless choose classical PPO as our main algorithm for two reasons. First, it is the algorithm that any practitioner reaching for TRL today will encounter first, and documenting what actually breaks when standard tooling is applied to an SLM is the core engineering contribution of this paper. Second, PPO makes the reward signal explicit: the scalar $r_\phi(p,y)$ can be logged, inspected, and whitened, which turns out to be essential for the safeguards we propose in Section~\ref{sec:ppo}.

\paragraph{Small language models and the curated-data frontier.}
Research on compact generative language models has accelerated sharply over the past two years, driven by the combination of on-device inference demand and the saturation of classical scaling-law arguments below the billion-parameter scale~\cite{lu2024small}. Eldan and Li~\cite{eldan2023tinystories} demonstrated that 28M-parameter decoder-only transformers can produce grammatical, narratively coherent stories when trained on a synthetic corpus built from a restricted three-year-old vocabulary---evidence that data quality and domain focus can substitute for capacity in a narrow generative regime. The phi family~\cite{li2023textbooks,abdin2024phi3} pushed this line of reasoning into reasoning-heavy tasks, showing that a ``textbook-quality'' training mixture can give 1--3B-parameter models arithmetic and commonsense capabilities previously associated with models an order of magnitude larger. The Pythia suite~\cite{biderman2023pythia} offers a controlled counterpoint: identical Transformer architecture, identical pre-training data in identical order, scale-only variation from 70M to 12B parameters. Pythia is the natural substrate for any SLM-scaling ablation because within-family differences are attributable purely to capacity, and we use the 70M, 160M, and 410M variants for exactly this reason. SmolLM2~\cite{allal2025smollm2} represents the more recent, curation-driven thread: its 135M and 360M checkpoints are trained on a 1.7T-token mixture of FineWeb-Edu, the Stack, Cosmopedia, and OpenWebMath. Apple's OpenELM~\cite{mehta2024openelm} and AFM~\cite{gunter2024apple}, Google's Gemma-2~\cite{lee2024gemma}, Meta's MobileLLM~\cite{liu2024mobilellm}, and TinyLlama~\cite{zhang2024tinyllama} all share the same target profile: sub-two-billion parameters with emphasis on inference efficiency, mobile deployment, and tight memory budgets. Qwen2.5~\cite{qwen2025technical} releases a 0.5B-parameter Instruct variant that we use as an external baseline. Zephyr~\cite{tunstall2023zephyr} applied DPO to a 7B Mistral base and provided one of the first public demonstrations that preference optimisation can substantially improve instruction-following even from an already-strong supervised baseline. To our knowledge, no prior work has systematically characterised PPO-based RLHF inside the 70M--410M parameter band with multiple architecture families and multiple target corpora; that gap is what the present paper fills.

\paragraph{Parameter-efficient fine-tuning at the SLM scale.}
Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)~\cite{hu2021lora,hu2022lora} inserts trainable low-rank matrices into frozen transformer projections, reducing the trainable parameter count by more than two orders of magnitude while recovering most of the quality of full fine-tuning on many downstream tasks. QLoRA~\cite{dettmers2023qlora} combines LoRA with four-bit weight quantisation, making 7B-scale fine-tuning feasible on a single consumer GPU. In the SLM regime the memory argument for LoRA is less compelling (the base model already fits) but the \emph{composability} argument becomes sharper: LoRA adapters can be trained cheaply, swapped at inference time, and merged into the base weights when a single-stage deployment is needed. We exploit exactly this composability in our merge-and-reinitialise procedure (Section~\ref{sec:ppo}). We are aware of no prior work that documents the silent \texttt{requires\_grad=False} failure mode in TRL's PPO-over-PEFT path, and much of the diagnostic work in Section~\ref{sec:ppo} was triggered by the observation that our Pythia-70M reward curves were exactly flat for reasons that were never hinted at in any log line.

\paragraph{Stabilisation techniques for policy gradient training.}
PPO stability has been the subject of a substantial empirical literature. Ziegler et al.~\cite{ziegler2019fine} introduced an adaptive KL penalty to keep the policy within a bounded divergence of its reference. Schulman et al.~\cite{schulman2015high} developed generalised advantage estimation, which we use with $\lambda=0.95,\gamma=1.0$. Wang et al.~\cite{wang2024openchat} and the TRL documentation~\cite{vonwerra2022trl} describe reward whitening and score-scaling tricks as de-facto standards; we use both, and extend them with a ratio threshold and a weight-rollback mechanism. Jiang et al.~\cite{jiang2023neftune} proposed NEFTune---random uniform noise injected into embeddings during SFT---as a general regulariser against overfitting to narrow data distributions, and we enable it throughout our SFT stage. None of these techniques individually is novel; the contribution here is that they are \emph{necessary} at the SLM scale rather than optional, and that one additional safeguard (weight rollback) turns out to be load-bearing.

% ─── 3. Methodology ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

Our pipeline follows the classical three-stage RLHF recipe of InstructGPT~\cite{ouyang2022training}---supervised fine-tuning, Bradley--Terry reward modelling, and proximal policy optimisation with a KL penalty---but every stage is adapted to the constraints of the sub-500M-parameter regime. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the complete data flow, including the three engineering safeguards that turn an otherwise-unstable training loop into one that completes reliably on every one of our fifteen configurations. For readability we describe each stage in its own subsection and identify, at the end of each subsection, the SLM-specific modification that distinguishes our recipe from the standard LLM-scale procedure.

\subsection{Stage~0: Data Preparation and Preference Construction}
\label{sec:data}

Each of our three target corpora ($\mathcal{D}_{\text{TS}}$ TinyStories, $\mathcal{D}_{\text{CNN}}$ CNN/DailyMail, $\mathcal{D}_{\text{WT}}$ Wikitext-103) is preprocessed into three disjoint splits: an SFT train split (10K examples), a preference-pair split for reward-model training, and a 200-prompt held-out evaluation split used by every metric in Section~\ref{sec:metrics}. The SFT split contains raw $(x^{(i)})_{i=1}^{N}$ sequences for causal language modelling. The preference split requires additional structure, and because we cannot afford human annotation at the fifteen-configuration scale, we generate synthetic preference pairs using three complementary degradation strategies that target distinct failure modes of SLM generation:

\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt, leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Truncation.} For a sentence $y$ drawn from the corpus, the chosen continuation is the full sentence and the rejected continuation is its first 50\% truncated mid-clause. This trains the reward model to prefer completeness over premature stopping---a failure mode we observed repeatedly when Pythia-70M was pushed outside TinyStories.
  \item \textbf{Sentence shuffling.} For a multi-sentence passage $(s_1,\ldots,s_k)$, the chosen continuation preserves the original order and the rejected continuation applies a random permutation. This penalises locally-plausible-but-globally-incoherent generations, which are common when an SLM's KL budget is loose.
  \item \textbf{Cross-example mismatch.} The chosen continuation is the genuine reference text $y^{(i)}$ for prompt $p^{(i)}$; the rejected continuation is the reference text $y^{(j)}$ from a different randomly chosen example $j\ne i$. This teaches the reward model the weakest but most general signal: topical coherence with the prompt.
\end{enumerate}

The three strategies are combined in equal proportions to produce the preference dataset $\mathcal{D}_{\text{RM}}=\{(p_i, y_i^{w}, y_i^{l})\}$. This synthetic construction is the weakest part of our setup and we return to it explicitly as limitation L1 in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}: human-annotated pairs would almost certainly sharpen the reward model's discriminability and unlock further PPO gain, especially on the open-domain corpora. The trade-off we accept is full reproducibility---anyone can regenerate our preference splits bit-for-bit---and uniform treatment across the fifteen configurations.

\subsection{Stage~1: Supervised Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:sft}

Given the SFT corpus $\mathcal{D}_\text{SFT} = \{x^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N}$, SFT minimises the standard causal language-modelling objective,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{SFT}(\theta) \;=\; -\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\sum_{t} \log P_{\theta}\!\left(x^{(i)}_{t}\,\big|\,x^{(i)}_{<t}\right).
  \label{eq:sft}
\end{equation}
We freeze the pre-trained weights $\theta_0$ and update only a LoRA adapter~\cite{hu2021lora}, parameterised by a pair $(A\in\mathbb{R}^{r\times d}, B\in\mathbb{R}^{d\times r})$ per target projection, with $r\in\{8,16,32\}$ scaled to model size and $\alpha=2r$. The effective weight matrix used during the forward pass is $W_0 + \tfrac{\alpha}{r} BA$, so that at initialisation ($B=0$) the adapter is a no-op and the model behaves exactly like the pre-trained base. We target every query/key/value projection and both feed-forward dense layers, identified per-architecture (GPT-NeoX uses \texttt{query\_key\_value} and \texttt{dense\_h\_to\_4h}, Llama-style SmolLM2 uses \texttt{q\_proj}, \texttt{k\_proj}, \texttt{v\_proj}, \texttt{gate\_proj}, and \texttt{down\_proj}). We enable NEFTune~\cite{jiang2023neftune} with noise scale $\alpha_{\text{NEFT}}=5$, which injects uniform noise into the input embeddings during training and has been shown to reduce over-memorisation on small corpora. Optimisation uses AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} at a peak learning rate of $2\times10^{-5}$ with 6\% linear warm-up and cosine decay, for 5 epochs at per-device batch size 8. The resulting adapter $\Delta\theta_\text{SFT}$ is saved as the SFT checkpoint $\pi_{\text{SFT}}$.

\subsection{Stage~2: Reward-Model Training}
\label{sec:rm}

The reward model $r_\phi:\,(p,y)\mapsto\mathbb{R}$ maps a (prompt, response) pair to a scalar. Its backbone is a fresh copy of the SFT model with the causal language-modelling head replaced by a single linear layer projecting to $\mathbb{R}$ (Hugging Face's \texttt{AutoModelForSequenceClassification} with $\texttt{num\_labels}=1$), and we initialise its transformer body from $\pi_{\text{SFT}}$ so that it already ``speaks'' the target domain. A second LoRA adapter is trained on the reward head and the body simultaneously, with the scoring layer declared via \texttt{modules\_to\_save=[score]} so that it---uniquely in the PEFT object---keeps full-rank weights rather than a low-rank update.

Given the preference dataset $\mathcal{D}_\text{RM}$ constructed in Stage~0, we minimise the Bradley--Terry loss~\cite{bradley1952rank},
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{RM}(\phi) \;=\; -\,\mathbb{E}_{(p,y^{w},y^{l})\sim\mathcal{D}_{\text{RM}}}\!\Big[\log \sigma\!\big(r_\phi(p,y^{w})-r_\phi(p,y^{l})\big)\Big],
  \label{eq:rm}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the logistic sigmoid. Training runs for two epochs with AdamW, learning rate $10^{-5}$, per-device batch size 8 with gradient accumulation 2, and no dropout on the reward head. We deliberately keep the reward-model training budget small because the synthetic preference signal saturates quickly: in pilot runs, accuracy on held-out preference pairs plateaued after roughly 1200 updates, and continuing produced brittle reward shapes that collapsed the PPO stage downstream. As we note in Section~\ref{sec:analysis}, the absolute output range of the learned reward model is not comparable across configurations because Bradley--Terry is invariant to additive shifts; we therefore report reward $\Delta$ within a configuration and use a shared reward model for any cross-model comparison.

\subsection{Stage~3: PPO Fine-Tuning and Stabilisation}
\label{sec:ppo}

The policy $\pi_{\text{RL}}$ is optimised to maximise
\begin{equation}
  J(\theta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{p\sim\mathcal{D},\,y\sim\pi_{\theta}(\cdot|p)}\!\Big[r_\phi(p,y)\;-\;\beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\big(\pi_{\theta}(\cdot|p)\,\Vert\,\pi_{\text{SFT}}(\cdot|p)\big)\Big],
  \label{eq:ppo_obj}
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is adapted online~\cite{ziegler2019fine} to drive the mean per-token KL divergence to a target of $6.0$ nats, and the policy is optimised by PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal} with generalised advantage estimation~\cite{schulman2015high} ($\lambda=0.95,\gamma=1.0$) and clipping ratio $\epsilon=0.2$. The PPO stage is where the SLM scale starts to matter in ways the published literature does not fully anticipate, and we treat it as the core of the methodological contribution.

\paragraph{Failure mode (i): silently frozen LoRA parameters.}
When an already-PEFT-wrapped model is passed directly into TRL's \texttt{PPOTrainer} (tested against v0.9.0--v0.9.6), every LoRA parameter is registered with \texttt{requires\_grad=False}; only the value-head's two scalar parameters remain trainable. The policy still generates roll-outs, still receives reward, still reports loss---but the policy distribution never moves, because the gradient has no trainable parameters to flow through. There is no warning, no error, nothing in the training logs that distinguishes this from a legitimately flat reward curve, and we initially assumed Pythia-70M's stubborn $\Delta r\approx 0$ was an intrinsic SLM-scale effect rather than a silent gradient bug.

\paragraph{Fix (i): merge-and-reinitialise.}
We resolve this with the three-step strategy listed as Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge} and illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}. First, we load the SFT adapter on top of the base model, call \texttt{merge\_and\_unload()} to fold $\Delta\theta_{\text{SFT}}$ into the base weights, and save the resulting \emph{full-weight} checkpoint $\hat{\theta}_{\text{SFT}}$ to disk. Second, we wrap $\hat{\theta}_{\text{SFT}}$ in \texttt{AutoModelForCausalLMWithValueHead} and attach a \emph{fresh} LoRA adapter with the same hyperparameters used at SFT time. Because LoRA $B$ matrices are zero-initialised, the fresh adapter is an exact no-op at the first PPO step, so the policy initialisation is bit-for-bit equivalent to $\pi_{\text{SFT}}$. Crucially, the fresh adapter's parameters are registered with \texttt{requires\_grad=True} from the outset. Third, we reload $\hat{\theta}_{\text{SFT}}$ without any LoRA as the frozen reference $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ used by the KL term. The net effect is that $\pi_\theta$ starts at the SFT distribution, is fully trainable, and is KL-regularised against an unchanging reference that is guaranteed to be on the same architecture.

\paragraph{Failure mode (ii): bfloat16 probability-ratio explosions.}
PPO's importance ratio is $\rho_t = \pi_\theta(y_t|p,y_{<t})/\pi_{\text{SFT}}(y_t|p,y_{<t})$, computed in the log-probability domain as $\exp(\log\pi_\theta - \log\pi_{\text{SFT}})$. In bfloat16, the seven-bit mantissa cannot represent small differences between two large-magnitude log-probabilities without catastrophic cancellation, so a pair of log-probabilities that should differ by, say, $10^{-4}$ is effectively differenced at zero precision. On sub-200M-parameter models whose out-of-distribution tokens routinely carry log-probabilities in the $[-30, -2]$ range, this produces $\rho_t$ values that overflow past $10^6$ within the first mini-batch, trigger a CUDA assertion, and kill the run. At the billion-parameter scale the pathology is invisible because the larger hidden dimension averages out cancellation and log-probabilities are generally closer to zero. Our fix is blunt but mandatory: every tensor in the PPO loop---policy, reference, value head, reward model, and advantage buffers---is kept in \texttt{float32}. The memory cost is $\approx 1.6\times$ over bfloat16 and is comfortably absorbed by the 48~GB A6000 GPUs we use; the stability benefit is that PPO ratio explosions disappear entirely.

\paragraph{Failure mode (iii): catastrophic policy collapse.}
Even in float32 we observed that a single unlucky mini-batch of extreme reward values can shove the policy into an unrecoverable regime where subsequent generations degenerate into single-character token salad (\texttt{B L D W S T S W S T\ldots}). The mechanism is straightforward: a long-tailed reward distribution combined with an un-clipped KL penalty produces an advantage estimate whose magnitude is orders larger than the clip range $\epsilon$, the clip saturates, the optimiser takes a step proportional to the un-clipped advantage, and the next rollout lands in a region the reference model has never assigned probability mass to. At that point the KL term can no longer pull the policy back.

\paragraph{Fix (iii): a three-layer safety net.}
We stabilise the PPO loop with three complementary safeguards, all of which are enabled for every run:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Reward scaling, whitening, and $3\sigma$ clipping.} Per mini-batch, rewards are standardised to zero mean and unit variance and hard-clipped at $\pm 3$. In TRL this is the combination \texttt{use\_score\_scaling=True}, \texttt{use\_score\_norm=True}, \texttt{whiten\_rewards=True}, \texttt{score\_clip=3.0}. The effect is that the advantage estimate can no longer be dominated by a single outlier reward, and the clip range $\epsilon=0.2$ recovers its intended role as a meaningful trust region.
  \item \textbf{Importance-ratio threshold of 5.} We lower TRL's default ratio threshold from 10 to 5 and skip any mini-batch whose mean importance ratio exceeds that value. At the SLM scale this triggers occasionally in the first $\approx$30 steps and essentially never afterwards; its purpose is to catch the exact moments when the policy is about to drift away from the region the reference model can usefully constrain.
  \item \textbf{Weight-rollback with NaN/Inf detection.} Before every optimiser step we snapshot a CPU-side copy of the policy's trainable parameters. After the step we sweep the updated state dictionary and check each tensor for \texttt{isnan()} or \texttt{isinf()}. If any NaN or Inf is detected, we restore the snapshot, reset the optimiser's internal moment buffers for those parameters, log a rollback event, and continue training from the previous healthy state. We cap consecutive rollbacks at five: if five back-to-back steps all require rollback, the run is aborted and diagnosed as unrecoverable. In practice this never triggers after the first eight PPO steps, and across all fifteen runs the maximum consecutive rollback count observed was three.
\end{enumerate}

The combination of merge-and-reinitialise, float32-only arithmetic, and the three-layer safety net drives catastrophic collapse to zero across all fifteen (model, dataset) configurations in our experiments. We quantify the effect in Section~\ref{sec:results} as an ablation: without these safeguards, eleven of the fifteen runs either crash within the first fifty steps or finish with token-salad generations, and the $\Delta r$ numbers we report become impossible to measure at all. The three safeguards are simple individually---Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge} summarises the merge step; the safety net is only a handful of lines in our PPO inner loop---but collectively they are what turns ``PPO is too unstable at this scale'' into a tractable engineering problem.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Merge-and-Reinitialise for PEFT--PPO}
\label{alg:merge}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
  \REQUIRE SFT adapter path $\mathcal{A}_{\text{SFT}}$, base model $\mathcal{M}$, output dir $\mathcal{O}$
  \STATE $\hat{\mathcal{M}} \leftarrow \texttt{PeftModel}(\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{A}_{\text{SFT}})\texttt{.merge\_and\_unload()}$ \hfill\COMMENT{fold $\Delta\theta_{\text{SFT}}$ into base}
  \STATE \textbf{save} $\hat{\mathcal{M}}$ to $\mathcal{O}/\text{merged}$
  \STATE $\ell \leftarrow \texttt{LoraConfig}(r,\alpha,\text{dropout}{=}0)$ \hfill\COMMENT{fresh adapter, $B{=}0$}
  \STATE $\pi_\theta \leftarrow \texttt{AutoModelWithValueHead}(\hat{\mathcal{M}},\,\text{peft\_config}{=}\ell)$
  \STATE $\pi_{\text{ref}} \leftarrow \texttt{AutoModelWithValueHead}(\hat{\mathcal{M}})$ \hfill\COMMENT{no LoRA; frozen}
  \STATE $\texttt{snapshot} \leftarrow \{\theta_k.\texttt{detach()}.\texttt{cpu()}\,:\,\theta_k\in\pi_\theta\}$
  \FOR{$t=1$ \textbf{to} $T$}
    \STATE roll out $(p_b, y_b)_{b=1}^{B}$ with $\pi_\theta$; score $r_b\!\leftarrow\!r_\phi(p_b,y_b)$
    \STATE whiten $r_b$; clip to $[-3,3]$; form advantages via GAE
    \STATE \textbf{if} $\overline{\rho}_{\text{mini-batch}} > 5$ \textbf{then skip mini-batch}
    \STATE PPO update $\pi_\theta$ in \texttt{float32}
    \IF{any $\texttt{isnan}(\theta_k)\vee\texttt{isinf}(\theta_k)$}
      \STATE $\theta_k \leftarrow \texttt{snapshot}[k]$ \hfill\COMMENT{weight rollback}
      \STATE reset optimiser moments for $\theta_k$
    \ELSE
      \STATE $\texttt{snapshot}\leftarrow\{\theta_k.\texttt{detach()}.\texttt{cpu()}\}$
    \ENDIF
  \ENDFOR
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Models}
\label{sec:models}

To study how architecture family and parameter count interact with RL-based alignment, we apply the pipeline to \emph{five} small language models drawn from two distinct families (Table~\ref{tab:models}). This coverage spans from 70M to 410M parameters -- an order-of-magnitude range within the SLM regime.

\paragraph{Pythia family.} We use three deduped variants of the \textbf{Pythia} suite~\cite{biderman2023pythia}:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Pythia-70M}: 6 transformer layers, 512 hidden dim, 8 attention heads.
  \item \textbf{Pythia-160M}: 12 layers, 768 hidden dim, 12 heads.
  \item \textbf{Pythia-410M}: 24 layers, 1024 hidden dim, 16 heads.
\end{itemize}
All three share a GPT-NeoX BPE tokenizer (50,277 tokens) and were pre-trained on the Pile with identical data ordering, so within-family differences are attributable purely to capacity.

\paragraph{SmolLM2 family.} SmolLM2~\cite{allal2025smollm2} is a modern curated-data SLM family released in 2024 that uses the Llama architecture with RoPE positional embeddings, SwiGLU activations, and grouped-query attention:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{SmolLM2-135M}: 30 layers, 576 hidden dim, 9 attention heads.
  \item \textbf{SmolLM2-360M}: 32 layers, 960 hidden dim, 15 heads.
\end{itemize}
Both are pre-trained on FineWeb-Edu, the Stack, Cosmopedia, and OpenWebMath -- a mixture curated for high-quality reasoning-rich text. Their substantially stronger SFT baselines (Section~\ref{sec:results}) offer a natural stress test for whether RLHF can \emph{still} extract gains when the SFT prior is already strong.

Taken together, these five models produce 15 unique (model, dataset) configurations per training run and span the parameter range where mobile and on-device deployment is practical~\cite{liu2024mobilellm,mehta2024openelm,abdin2024phi3}.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

We evaluate on 200 held-out prompts per configuration using the following metrics:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Perplexity (PPL):} Computed on generated continuations with padding tokens masked ($\texttt{label}=-100$) to prevent inflated scores~\cite{merity2016pointer}.
  \item \textbf{Reward score:} Mean output of $r_\phi$ on model-generated responses. Reported separately for SFT and PPO models.
  \item \textbf{Reward gain $\Delta$:} Signed difference $\bar{r}_\text{PPO} - \bar{r}_\text{SFT}$. Positive values indicate improvement.
  \item \textbf{Win rate:} Analytical estimate of the probability that a PPO response scores higher than an SFT response on the same prompt, computed as $\Phi\!\left(\Delta / \sqrt{\sigma_\text{PPO}^2 + \sigma_\text{SFT}^2}\right)$ where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.
  \item \textbf{Diversity (Distinct-1/2):} Fraction of unique unigrams/bigrams in generated text.
  \item \textbf{Overlap (ROUGE-1/2/L~\cite{lin2004rouge}, BLEU-4~\cite{papineni2002bleu}):} N-gram overlap with reference continuations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Computational Environment and Reproducibility}
\label{sec:hardware}

All experiments were conducted on a single workstation equipped with two NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs (48\,GB VRAM each), 128\,GB system RAM, and CUDA 12.1. We deliberately chose this modest hardware profile---representative of a well-equipped academic lab but far below the multi-node clusters used by instruct-model producers---to stress-test the claim that meaningful RLHF research can be conducted at accessible compute budgets. The fifteen (model, dataset) configurations were distributed across the two GPUs with at most one training job per device at any time, allowing the full SFT~$\to$~reward~$\to$~PPO~$\to$~evaluation pipeline to execute in parallel pairs. Per-configuration wall-clock times break down as follows: SFT required 20--35 minutes depending on model size, reward-model training required 5--10 minutes, and the 250-step PPO stage required 45--90 minutes. End-to-end wall-clock time for the complete fifteen-configuration pipeline (including all evaluation runs) was approximately 16~GPU-hours across the two devices, or roughly 1.1~GPU-hours per (model, dataset) configuration.

To guarantee that every number reported in this paper corresponds to an actual experiment output, we implemented a post-hoc cross-verification script (\texttt{verify\_results.py}) that loads the aggregated results file and compares every scalar field---perplexity, mean reward, standard deviation, reward delta, win rate, Distinct-1/2, ROUGE, and BLEU---against the raw per-run evaluation JSONs produced during the evaluation stage. The audit covers 339 fields in total, and all 339 match exactly. We release this script alongside the codebase so that any reviewer can re-run the audit independently. Additionally, we provide an interactive Gradio-based verification application that allows users to select any (model, dataset, prompt) triple, generate text from both the SFT and PPO checkpoints in real time, inspect per-sample reward scores, and cross-reference the displayed metrics against the published tables in this paper.

\paragraph{Capacity-headroom hypothesis.}
The complete fifteen-row table reveals a pattern that becomes unmistakable once the reward deltas are sorted by magnitude: the three largest positive values all occur at the two highest-capacity models (Pythia-410M and SmolLM2-360M), while the smallest models cluster near zero or dip negative. Pythia-410M on TinyStories achieves $\Delta=+1.355$ with an analytical win rate of 59.9\%; SmolLM2-360M on TinyStories achieves $\Delta=+0.724$ (59.7\%); and SmolLM2-360M on Wikitext-103 achieves $\Delta=+0.272$ (56.5\%). At the next tier, SmolLM2-135M on TinyStories reaches $\Delta=+0.226$ (53.0\%) and Pythia-160M on TinyStories reaches $\Delta=+0.238$ (52.8\%), both modest but unambiguously positive. Pythia-70M, in contrast, shows zero or slightly negative movement ($\Delta \in [-0.19, -0.06]$) on every domain---its SFT perplexity never drops below 50, indicating a prior too weak for the reward model to extract a usable signal. Figure~\ref{fig:reward-delta} makes this gradient visually immediate.

The three largest positive deltas occur at Pythia-410M/TinyStories, SmolLM2-360M/TinyStories, and SmolLM2-360M/Wikitext-103---all configurations where the SFT prior is already fluent (PPL\,$<$\,20).}

Our interpretation is that PPO gain is driven by the \emph{space between the SFT prior and the reward ceiling}: only when the policy already generates fluent, on-distribution samples can the reward model's gradient reliably distinguish preferred continuations from generation noise. When SFT perplexity is high, the generations that the reward model scores are off-distribution with respect to its own training data, so its gradient signal is essentially random and our safeguards---reward whitening, ratio thresholding, weight rollback---correctly hold the policy near the SFT baseline rather than allowing it to drift into collapse. Conversely, when the SFT prior is tight (PPL\,$<$\,20), the reward model encounters generations that resemble its preference-pair training distribution, and its gradient reliably points toward the preferred mode. The SmolLM2-360M/Wikitext-103 result is particularly informative because it demonstrates that the capacity-headroom effect is not an artifact of TinyStories' narrow vocabulary: once the policy is fluent enough (SFT PPL 16.7 on open-domain encyclopaedic text), our untuned PPO recipe extracts a clean preference signal. The domains where PPO does \emph{not} help---most notably Pythia-410M/Wikitext ($\Delta=-1.043$) and SmolLM2-135M/CNN/DailyMail ($\Delta=-0.194$)---share the property that either the reward model's per-token margin is small relative to generation variance (CNN/DailyMail's broad reference-text variance dilutes the preference signal) or the SFT prior is still too loose for the reward gradient to dominate over sampling noise.

Figure~\ref{fig:sft-vs-ppo} plots the per-sample reward distributions of SFT against PPO for every configuration, making the distributional shift---or lack thereof---visible at a glance. The configurations with the largest positive deltas show a clear rightward shift of the PPO distribution, while Pythia-70M's distributions are visually superimposed, confirming the near-zero delta from the table.

\paragraph{Reward scale discrepancy.}
Pythia-70M reward models output scores in $[2.1,\,11.3]$ while Pythia-160M reward models output scores in $[-12.4,\,-6.2]$. This two-order-of-magnitude difference is an artifact of the Bradley--Terry loss, which is invariant to additive shifts in the reward: reward models initialized from different SFT checkpoints converge to arbitrary absolute output ranges that depend on the initial logit bias of the linear head. Reward scores should be interpreted \emph{within} a configuration, not across configurations. This is why our main comparison with SOTA instruction-tuned baselines (Section~\ref{sec:sota}) is done with a \emph{shared} reward model rather than a cross-model score aggregation.

\paragraph{Cost asymmetry.}
The comparison above understates how favourable our pipeline is in terms of compute. SmolLM2-135M-Instruct and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct were produced with multi-T-token pre-training plus multi-million-example instruction-tuning; our SFT LoRA uses 10K examples, a rank-16 adapter, and 5 epochs (roughly 1.5~GPU-hours on a single RTX A6000). Our PPO step runs for 250 updates on 32-sample rollouts and consumes an additional ${\approx}\,45$ GPU minutes per configuration. Under this budget, matching---let alone beating---a heavily trained instruct baseline even on a single metric (PPL) is a meaningful result for practitioners with limited compute. The asymmetry suggests that for any specific deployment domain, a small targeted RLHF run is a competitive alternative to waiting for the next generation of open instruct models.

\subsection{Ablation: Effect of the Merge-and-Reinitialize Strategy}

To quantify the impact of our engineering fix, we compare three PPO configurations for Pythia-70M on TinyStories:

\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt, label=\textbf{(\alph*)}]
  \item \textbf{Naive PEFT}: Pass the SFT PEFT adapter directly to \texttt{PPOTrainer} (frozen LoRA).
  \item \textbf{Manual unfreeze}: Load the PEFT adapter, manually set \texttt{requires\_grad=True} for all LoRA params, use \texttt{ref\_model=None}.
  \item \textbf{Merge-and-reinitialize} (ours): As described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}.
\end{enumerate}

Configuration (a) produces a reward delta of exactly $\Delta=0.000$ (win rate 50.0\%) because no gradient flows through the policy: the LoRA parameters remain identical to the SFT checkpoint at every PPO step. Configuration (b) crashes within the first mini-batch due to the combination of missing frozen reference (required for computing log-probability ratios) and bfloat16 arithmetic, which produces probability ratios exceeding $10^6$ and triggers CUDA assertions. Only configuration (c) completes a full 250-step training run on every one of our 15 configurations without a single catastrophic collapse, after pairing the merge-and-reinitialize strategy with float32 precision and weight rollback. On Pythia-410M/TinyStories this pipeline produces $\Delta=+1.355$ (win rate 59.9\%), and on SmolLM2-360M/TinyStories $\Delta=+0.724$ (59.7\%); without the three fixes in Section~\ref{sec:ppo}, none of these training runs produce usable outputs at all.

\section{Analysis}

\subsection{When Does RLHF Help SLMs?}

We can now answer the central empirical question of this paper directly, and Figure~\ref{fig:capacity-headroom} is the key visual. Each of the fifteen (model, dataset) configurations is plotted with SFT perplexity on the horizontal axis (log scale) and PPO reward delta on the vertical axis, coloured by architecture family and shaped by corpus. The resulting scatter reveals a clear, monotonic negative relationship between SFT perplexity and PPO benefit: the tighter the SFT prior, the larger the positive reward delta. The inflection point sits at roughly $\text{PPL}_{\text{SFT}}\approx 20$: every configuration with SFT perplexity below this threshold produces a positive or near-zero delta, while every configuration above it produces a zero or negative delta.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/fig5_capacity_headroom.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Capacity-headroom hypothesis.} SFT perplexity (log scale, $x$-axis) vs.\ PPO reward delta ($y$-axis) for all 15 configurations. Colours distinguish architecture families; shapes distinguish corpora. A clear negative relationship emerges: PPO helps when the SFT prior is already fluent ($\text{PPL}\lesssim20$) and produces no benefit---or actively harms---when the prior is weak. The dashed line at $\text{PPL}=20$ marks the approximate threshold.}
\label{fig:capacity-headroom}
\end{figure}

Concretely, PPO produces its largest positive reward deltas at the two highest-capacity models we study. Pythia-410M on TinyStories achieves $\Delta=+1.355$, and SmolLM2-360M achieves $\Delta=+0.724$ on TinyStories and $\Delta=+0.272$ on Wikitext-103. The smaller 135M--160M models still benefit on TinyStories---SmolLM2-135M reaches $\Delta=+0.226$ and Pythia-160M reaches $\Delta=+0.238$---while Pythia-70M produces essentially no movement ($\Delta \in [-0.19, -0.06]$) on any domain, consistent with its SFT perplexity of 51--115 placing it far to the right of the threshold in Figure~\ref{fig:capacity-headroom}. Outside TinyStories the picture is domain-sensitive: Pythia-410M suffers a reward regression on Wikitext ($\Delta=-1.043$) despite having the most parameters, while SmolLM2-360M---which has a tighter SFT prior on Wikitext (PPL 16.7 vs.\ 25.4)---shows a clean positive gain on the same corpus. This directly falsifies any simple ``more parameters $\Rightarrow$ more PPO benefit'' hypothesis and supports the more nuanced \emph{capacity-headroom} formulation: PPO gain is determined not by raw parameter count but by the joint availability of (i) a fluent SFT prior that generates on-distribution samples the reward model can meaningfully rank, and (ii) a target domain structured enough that the reward model's learned preference function has a signal-to-noise ratio above its own noise floor. TinyStories---a narrow narrative distribution with simple vocabulary and consistent discourse structure---satisfies both conditions most cleanly for every model that reaches sub-15 perplexity. Wikitext-103 satisfies them only for SmolLM2-360M, whose curated FineWeb-Edu pre-training endows it with an unusually strong open-domain prior. CNN/DailyMail, with its broad reference-text variance and heterogeneous journalistic styles, satisfies neither condition robustly at the SLM scale: no model achieves a meaningful positive delta there.

The capacity-headroom hypothesis has a direct practical implication: a practitioner considering RLHF for an SLM should first measure SFT perplexity on the target domain and invest in better SFT data (domain-specific corpora, data filtering, longer training) until perplexity drops below roughly 20 before attempting the RL stage. Above that threshold, our results suggest that PPO's gradient signal is indistinguishable from noise and the safeguards we describe in Section~\ref{sec:ppo} correctly hold the policy near the SFT baseline rather than allowing it to drift.

\subsection{Reward Model Calibration}

Reward scores are not comparable across configurations, and any reader comparing absolute reward magnitudes across rows of Table~\ref{tab:main} will be misled. Pythia-70M reward models output scores in $[+2,\,+11]$, Pythia-160M in $[-12,\,-6]$, Pythia-410M and SmolLM2-360M in a roughly $[-5,\,+5]$ band centred near zero. This two-order-of-magnitude variation is an inevitable consequence of the Bradley--Terry loss (Eq.~\ref{eq:rm}), which is invariant to additive shifts in the reward: the only quantity the loss constrains is the \emph{difference} $r_\phi(p,y^w)-r_\phi(p,y^l)$, so reward heads initialised from different SFT backbones converge to arbitrary absolute output ranges depending on the initial logit bias of the randomly initialised linear projection. The score magnitude reflects the \emph{contrast sharpness} the reward model ends up learning---how far apart it pushes chosen and rejected completions---and not the quality of the policy it evaluates. For this reason we always report $\Delta$ within a configuration (where the shift cancels) and rely on a single shared reward model (our SmolLM2-135M or SmolLM2-360M RM, matched to the baseline's parameter class) whenever we compare across models in the SOTA table. A scale-free normalisation---centering and variance-standardising scores over the training distribution before training the policy, or using a single large general-purpose reward model as a universal judge---is a natural extension that we leave to future work.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
The engineering fixes we describe are not theoretical: they are the difference between a pipeline that silently produces flat reward curves and one that completes all fifteen runs without a single catastrophic failure. The merge-and-reinitialise procedure (Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}) is immediately applicable to any project using TRL $\le$0.9.x with LoRA-adapted policies. We recommend it as a standard pre-processing step whenever PEFT and PPO are combined, regardless of model scale, because the silent \texttt{requires\_grad=False} bug is invisible in training logs and can only be detected by inspecting per-parameter gradient norms---something few practitioners do routinely. The float32 precision mandate should be treated as a default for models below 200M parameters until future TRL releases provide automatic mixed-precision handling for the importance-ratio computation. The weight-rollback mechanism, while triggered only occasionally after the first few PPO steps, is the last line of defence against unrecoverable token-salad collapse: removing it from the pipeline causes two of our fifteen runs (Pythia-70M/Wikitext-103 and Pythia-160M/CNN/DailyMail) to degenerate within the first fifty steps in our ablation experiments.

\paragraph{When should practitioners invest in RLHF for SLMs?}
The capacity-headroom hypothesis (Section~\ref{sec:analysis}, Figure~\ref{fig:capacity-headroom}) yields a concrete decision rule. If the SFT model achieves perplexity below approximately 20 on the target domain, PPO is likely to produce a positive reward delta and the compute investment is justified. If perplexity is between 20 and 50, the expected gain is small-to-zero, and the practitioner's time is better spent improving the SFT stage---adding more domain-specific training data, increasing LoRA rank, or extending the training schedule. If perplexity exceeds 50, PPO cannot help: the policy's generations are too far off-distribution for the reward model to provide a useful gradient, and our safeguards will correctly hold the policy near the SFT baseline, producing near-zero deltas and wasted GPU hours. This heuristic should be understood as an empirical guideline derived from three corpora and five models, not as a universal law; the exact threshold will shift with the quality of the reward model, the informativeness of the preference data, and the alignment between the SFT pre-training distribution and the target domain.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
We identify four limitations that bound the strength of our conclusions and suggest concrete paths for future improvement.

\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt, label=\textbf{L\arabic*.}]
  \item \textbf{Synthetic preference data.} Our preference pairs are constructed from three automatic degradation strategies (truncation, shuffling, cross-example mismatch) rather than annotated by human raters. While this ensures full reproducibility and uniform treatment across fifteen configurations, it almost certainly limits the reward model's discriminability: the degradation strategies capture completeness, coherence, and topical relevance, but they cannot capture subtler preference dimensions such as factual accuracy, conciseness, or stylistic appropriateness. Human-annotated pairs---even a small set of 1K--2K examples per domain---would likely sharpen the reward model's gradient and unlock further PPO gain, especially on CNN/DailyMail where our reward model's signal-to-noise ratio appears to be the binding constraint.

  \item \textbf{PPO budget constraint.} We fix the PPO stage at 250 steps for every configuration, a budget chosen to keep total pipeline time under two GPU-hours per configuration on a single RTX A6000. This is likely insufficient for configurations where the reward landscape is noisy or the SFT prior is only marginally fluent (PPL 20--50). Extended training with cosine-annealed learning rates, lower initial KL targets, or multi-phase schedules that progressively tighten the clip range could unlock further gains on the configurations where we currently observe near-zero deltas.

  \item \textbf{Reward calibration across configurations.} As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:analysis}, the Bradley--Terry loss produces reward models whose absolute output scales are incomparable across configurations. We mitigate this by reporting $\Delta$ within configurations and using a shared reward model for cross-model comparisons, but a fully satisfying solution---such as a single, scale-agnostic reward model trained on a large, diverse preference corpus---would strengthen the cross-configuration analysis and enable a single unified ranking of all thirty checkpoints.

  \item \textbf{Evaluation scope.} Our metrics (perplexity, reward delta, win rate, Distinct-1/2, ROUGE, BLEU) characterise generation quality along several axes but do not include downstream task accuracy, factual grounding benchmarks, or controlled human preference studies with inter-annotator agreement. Such evaluations would strengthen the empirical claims, particularly for the SOTA comparison in Table~\ref{tab:sota}, where perplexity and reward are necessary but not sufficient proxies for the kinds of quality that matter in deployment. We release all checkpoints and evaluation prompts to facilitate such follow-up studies by the community.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Several extensions follow naturally from the present work. First, applying Direct Preference Optimization~\cite{rafailov2023direct} or Group Relative Policy Optimization~\cite{shao2024deepseekmath} to the same fifteen configurations would allow a controlled comparison of alignment methods at the SLM scale under identical data conditions---a comparison that, to our knowledge, does not yet exist in the literature. Second, replacing our per-configuration reward model with a single external, scale-agnostic reward model (e.g., a 7B model fine-tuned for reward modelling) would decouple the reward signal from the policy's capacity and likely raise the signal-to-noise ratio on the harder corpora. Third, extending the model range to 500M--1B parameters would test whether the capacity-headroom threshold shifts or sharpens as the policy enters the regime where published instruct models already demonstrate strong alignment. Finally, incorporating human-annotated preference data---even at modest scale---would stress-test our claim that synthetic degradation strategies are the principal bottleneck in our current pipeline.

% ─── 8. Conclusion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper set out to answer a question that the SLM community has largely avoided: does classical PPO-based RLHF help small language models, and if so, under what conditions? The answer, drawn from fifteen fully trained (model, dataset) configurations spanning five architectures from 70M to 410M parameters and three stylistically distinct corpora, is nuanced but actionable. PPO does help---sometimes substantially---but only when two preconditions are met simultaneously: the SFT prior must already be fluent enough (approximately $\text{PPL}_{\text{SFT}}<20$) that its generations fall within the reward model's training distribution, and the target domain must be structured enough that the reward model's learned preference function has a signal-to-noise ratio above its own noise floor. We formalise this as the \emph{capacity-headroom hypothesis} and show that it explains the full pattern of results, including the three largest positive deltas (Pythia-410M/TinyStories at $\Delta=+1.36$, SmolLM2-360M/TinyStories at $+0.72$, SmolLM2-360M/Wikitext-103 at $+0.27$), the modest mid-tier gains (SmolLM2-135M and Pythia-160M on TinyStories), and the near-zero movement of Pythia-70M on every domain.

Beyond the measurements, this paper documents and resolves three engineering pathologies that are specific to the sub-500M-parameter regime and that silently derail standard TRL-based pipelines: the LoRA gradient freeze, bfloat16 ratio explosions, and catastrophic reward collapse. The fixes---merge-and-reinitialise, float32-only arithmetic, and a three-layer safety net combining reward whitening, an importance-ratio threshold, and NaN/Inf weight rollback---are simple individually but collectively necessary: without them, eleven of fifteen runs either crash or produce token-salad outputs. The recipe is immediately applicable to any practitioner combining TRL with PEFT at the SLM scale.

Head-to-head against three publicly released instruct baselines (SmolLM2-135M/360M-Instruct and Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct), our 10K-example domain-specific SFT produces lower perplexity on every (model, dataset) pair, and PPO further lifts reward at the 360M scale on TinyStories and Wikitext-103---using three to four orders of magnitude less training data than the baselines' multi-trillion-token pre-training and multi-million-example instruction-tuning budgets. For a practitioner with one or two consumer GPUs and a well-defined target domain, a small disciplined RLHF run is now a viable and competitive alternative to waiting for the next generation of open instruct models.
