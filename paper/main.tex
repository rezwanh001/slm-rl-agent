% ─── Abstract ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) achieve strong performance across a wide range of natural language tasks, yet their enormous computational cost limits deployment in resource-constrained settings. In this work we study whether Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) can meaningfully improve small language models (SLMs) with 70M--160M parameters. We implement a three-stage pipeline---supervised fine-tuning (SFT), reward model training, and proximal policy optimization (PPO)---applied to two Pythia architectures across three diverse text corpora: TinyStories, CNN/DailyMail, and Wikitext-103. Beyond reporting empirical results, we document and resolve two critical engineering obstacles that arise specifically at the SLM scale: (1) TRL's PPO trainer silently freezes LoRA parameters when the policy is a PEFT adapter, preventing any learning; and (2) reduced-precision (bfloat16) arithmetic causes PPO probability-ratio explosions in very small models, requiring full float32 precision. We address both issues with a \emph{merge-and-reinitialize} strategy and precision selection. Our experiments reveal that RLHF benefits at the SLM scale are domain-sensitive: Pythia-70M achieves a positive reward gain on TinyStories (the simplest domain), while improvements on more complex corpora remain marginal. We release all trained model weights, preference datasets, evaluation code, and an interactive Gradio demonstration to support reproducible research at modest hardware budgets.
\end{abstract}

% ─── 1. Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The past several years have witnessed a dramatic expansion in the capabilities of generative language models~\cite{radford2019language,brown2020language,scao2022bloom}. Models exceeding 100 billion parameters now achieve near-human performance on many benchmarks, yet the compute required to train and serve such systems is out of reach for most organizations. This has motivated a growing body of research on \emph{small language models} (SLMs)---architectures with fewer than 500 million parameters---that can run efficiently on consumer hardware while still producing coherent text~\cite{eldan2023tinystories,biderman2023pythia,allal2024smollm}.

A key limitation of current SLMs is not raw language modeling ability but \emph{alignment}: the degree to which outputs are helpful, accurate, and preferred by human users. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF)~\cite{christiano2017deep} has become the dominant alignment paradigm at the large-model scale, underpinning systems such as InstructGPT~\cite{ouyang2022training}, Claude~\cite{bai2022training}, and Llama~2-Chat~\cite{touvron2023llama}. However, its systematic application to SLMs---and the engineering challenges unique to this regime---has received comparatively little attention.

This paper makes the following contributions:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{End-to-end RLHF at the SLM scale.} We apply a complete SFT $\to$ reward model $\to$ PPO pipeline to two Pythia architectures (70M and 160M parameters) across three text domains, providing one of the first systematic empirical characterizations of RLHF at this scale.

  \item \textbf{Merge-and-reinitialize for PEFT--PPO compatibility.} We identify that TRL~\cite{vonwerra2022trl} v0.9 freezes LoRA parameters when the policy is loaded as a PEFT model, and propose a straightforward fix: merge the SFT adapter into base weights, then attach a fresh LoRA before PPO training.

  \item \textbf{Float32 stability requirement.} We show empirically that bfloat16 causes PPO clipping-ratio explosions in models below 200M parameters, and document float32 as the required precision for stable training at this scale.

  \item \textbf{Domain-sensitivity analysis.} We analyze how the difficulty of the target domain interacts with model capacity to determine whether RLHF provides a measurable benefit, finding that a pre-condition of moderate SFT perplexity is necessary for PPO to improve rewards.

  \item \textbf{Open artifacts.} All trained checkpoints, preference datasets, training scripts, and an interactive comparison demo are publicly released.
\end{enumerate}

% ─── 2. Related Work ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{RLHF and alignment.}
Christiano et al.~\cite{christiano2017deep} introduced learning a reward function from pairwise human comparisons, applied first to robotics tasks and later scaled to language summarization by Stiennon et al.~\cite{stiennon2020learning}. Ouyang et al.~\cite{ouyang2022training} combined SFT, reward modeling, and PPO~\cite{schulman2017proximal} to produce InstructGPT, establishing RLHF as the standard alignment recipe. Recent variants include Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)~\cite{rafailov2023direct}, which eliminates the RL stage by treating the policy itself as an implicit reward, and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO)~\cite{shao2024deepseekmath}, which removes the critic network. Our work uses classical PPO because it provides an explicit reward signal and is well-supported in open tooling, making it the most accessible starting point for SLM practitioners.

\paragraph{Small language models.}
Eldan and Li~\cite{eldan2023tinystories} showed that models with as few as 28M parameters produce grammatically correct narratives when trained on a carefully curated synthetic corpus, revealing that data quality can partially compensate for reduced capacity. The Pythia suite~\cite{biderman2023pythia} provides a controlled family of decoder-only transformers from 70M to 12B parameters trained on the Pile with fixed data ordering, enabling clean ablations across scale. SmolLM2~\cite{allal2024smollm} pushed further by demonstrating that aggressive curation of a 1.7T-token training mixture could yield strong 135M and 360M parameter models. Zephyr~\cite{tunstall2023zephyr} applied DPO to a 7B Mistral model and showed that preference optimization can substantially improve instruction following even from strong baselines. We complement these works by focusing on the sub-200M regime and documenting the unique engineering challenges it introduces.

\paragraph{Parameter-efficient fine-tuning.}
Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)~\cite{hu2021lora} injects trainable low-rank matrices into frozen transformer layers, reducing the trainable parameter count by 99\%+ while matching full fine-tuning quality on many tasks. QLoRA~\cite{dettmers2023qlora} further combines LoRA with 4-bit weight quantization. These techniques are standard practice for SFT at the small-model scale. However, as we demonstrate in Section~\ref{sec:method}, applying LoRA adapters directly within TRL's PPO loop requires special handling to maintain gradient flow.

% ─── 3. Methodology ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

Our pipeline follows the standard three-stage RLHF recipe but addresses two technical issues that arise specifically when applying it to small models via PEFT. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} provides an overview.

\subsection{Stage 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning}

Given a pre-trained SLM with parameters $\theta_0$ and a domain corpus $\mathcal{D}_\text{SFT} = \{x^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{N}$, SFT minimizes the standard language modeling loss:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{SFT}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{t} \log P_\theta\!\left(x^{(i)}_t \mid x^{(i)}_{<t}\right).
  \label{eq:sft}
\end{equation}
We apply LoRA~\cite{hu2021lora} with rank $r=8$ and $\alpha=16$, targeting query/key/value projections and feed-forward dense layers. Training uses AdamW~\cite{loshchilov2019decoupled} with a peak learning rate of $2\times10^{-5}$, linear warmup over 6\% of steps, and cosine decay. We train for 3 epochs with per-device batch size 8. The resulting adapter is saved as the SFT checkpoint $\pi_\text{SFT}$.

\subsection{Stage 2: Reward Model Training}

The reward model $r_\phi$ maps a (prompt, response) pair to a scalar score. Its backbone mirrors the SFT model but with the language modeling head replaced by a single linear layer projecting to $\mathbb{R}$. We initialize from $\pi_\text{SFT}$ to leverage its domain representations.

Given a preference dataset $\mathcal{D}_\text{RM} = \{(p_i, y_i^w, y_i^l)\}$ where $y_i^w \succ y_i^l$ (chosen preferred over rejected), we minimize the Bradley--Terry loss~\cite{bradley1952rank}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\text{RM}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{(p,y^w,y^l)\sim\mathcal{D}_\text{RM}}\!\left[\log \sigma\!\left(r_\phi(p,y^w) - r_\phi(p,y^l)\right)\right],
  \label{eq:rm}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function. Training runs for a single epoch (learning rate $10^{-5}$, batch size 4) to avoid overfitting on the relatively small preference set.

\subsection{Stage 3: PPO Fine-Tuning}
\label{sec:ppo}

The policy $\pi_\text{RL}$ is optimized to maximize:
\begin{equation}
  J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p \sim \mathcal{D}, y \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot|p)}\!\!\left[r_\phi(p,y) - \beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\pi_\theta(\cdot|p)\,\|\,\pi_\text{SFT}(\cdot|p)\right)\right],
  \label{eq:ppo_obj}
\end{equation}
where $\beta$ is the KL penalty coefficient, adapted online~\cite{ziegler2019fine} to keep KL divergence near a target of 0.1 nats. We use PPO with generalized advantage estimation (GAE)~\cite{schulman2015high} and clipping ratio $\epsilon=0.2$.

\paragraph{Problem: frozen LoRA parameters in TRL PPO.}
When a PEFT-adapted model is passed directly to TRL's \texttt{PPOTrainer} (v0.9.x), all LoRA parameters are registered with \texttt{requires\_grad=False}. Only the value head (two scalar parameters) remains trainable. The policy generates text, receives reward, but the PPO gradient has no effect on the generation distribution. This silent failure is not indicated by any warning or error.

\paragraph{Solution: merge-and-reinitialize.}
We address this with a three-step strategy (Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}):
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item Merge the SFT LoRA adapter into the base model weights via \texttt{merge\_and\_unload()}, producing a full-weight checkpoint $\hat{\theta}_\text{SFT}$.
  \item Apply a \emph{fresh} LoRA to the merged checkpoint. Since LoRA B matrices are initialized to zero, the freshly-initialized adapter is a no-op, preserving the SFT distribution at the start of PPO.
  \item Use a copy of the merged checkpoint (without any LoRA) as the frozen reference $\pi_\text{SFT}$.
\end{enumerate}
This ensures that all LoRA B and A matrices are registered with \texttt{requires\_grad=True} from the start of PPO training.

\paragraph{Float32 precision.}
When we use bfloat16 for the 70M and 160M parameter models, PPO probability ratios regularly exceed $10^6$ within the first training batch, triggering CUDA assertions. We attribute this to the limited numerical dynamic range of bfloat16 ($\approx 3.4 \times 10^{38}$ vs.\ $\approx 1.8 \times 10^{308}$ for float32) compounding with the small log-probabilities characteristic of small models on out-of-distribution tokens. All PPO experiments use float32.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Merge-and-Reinitialize for PEFT--PPO}
\label{alg:merge}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
  \REQUIRE SFT adapter path $\mathcal{A}_\text{SFT}$, base model $\mathcal{M}$, output dir $\mathcal{O}$
  \STATE $\hat{\mathcal{M}} \leftarrow \texttt{PeftModel}(\mathcal{M}, \mathcal{A}_\text{SFT})\texttt{.merge\_and\_unload()}$
  \STATE \textbf{save} $\hat{\mathcal{M}}$ to $\mathcal{O}/\text{merged}$
  \STATE $\ell \leftarrow \texttt{LoraConfig}(r, \alpha, \text{dropout}=0)$ \hfill\COMMENT{fresh adapter}
  \STATE $\pi_\theta \leftarrow \texttt{AutoModelWithValueHead}(\hat{\mathcal{M}},\, \text{peft\_config}=\ell)$
  \STATE $\pi_\text{ref} \leftarrow \texttt{AutoModelWithValueHead}(\hat{\mathcal{M}})$ \hfill\COMMENT{no LoRA; frozen}
  \STATE \textbf{run PPO} with $(\pi_\theta,\, \pi_\text{ref},\, r_\phi)$ in \texttt{float32}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/pipeline.pdf}
  \caption{\textbf{Three-stage RLHF pipeline.} \emph{Stage 1 (SFT):} A LoRA adapter is trained on domain text; the adapter is saved as $\pi_\text{SFT}$. \emph{Stage 2 (Reward):} A reward model $r_\phi$ is initialized from $\pi_\text{SFT}$ and trained on pairwise preference data via the Bradley--Terry loss. \emph{Stage 3 (PPO):} The SFT adapter is merged into the base weights; a fresh LoRA $\pi_\theta$ and a frozen reference $\pi_\text{ref}$ are created from this merged model. \section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Models}

We use two architectures from the \textbf{Pythia} suite~\cite{biderman2023pythia}, both in their deduped variants:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Pythia-70M}: 6 transformer layers, 512 hidden dim, 8 attention heads, $\approx70$M parameters.
  \item \textbf{Pythia-160M}: 12 layers, 768 hidden dim, 12 heads, $\approx160$M parameters.
\end{itemize}

Both models share the same BPE tokenizer (50,277 vocabulary), were pre-trained on the same corpus (the Pile) with identical data ordering, and differ only in capacity. This makes them ideal for studying scaling effects in isolation.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

We select three corpora with distinct characteristics (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}):

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{TinyStories}~\cite{eldan2023tinystories}: Short, simple English narratives (vocabulary restricted to words a 3-year-old knows). Both Pythia models have low perplexity on this domain.
  \item \textbf{CNN/DailyMail}~\cite{nallapati2016abstractive}: News articles. The vocabulary is broader and the discourse is more formal, producing moderate perplexity for the 160M model and high perplexity for the 70M model.
  \item \textbf{Wikitext-103}~\cite{merity2016pointer}: Wikipedia articles with technical and encyclopedic content. This represents the hardest domain for both models.
\end{itemize}

For each dataset we use 10K examples for SFT and construct preference pairs for reward model training via three degradation strategies: \emph{truncation} (preferred = full sentence, rejected = first 50\%), \emph{sentence shuffling} (rejected = sentences in random order), and \emph{cross-example mismatch} (rejected = continuation from a different article). These synthetically constructed contrasts provide a clear training signal and enable full reproducibility without human annotation.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

We evaluate on 200 held-out prompts per configuration using the following metrics:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt]
  \item \textbf{Perplexity (PPL):} Computed on generated continuations with padding tokens masked ($\texttt{label}=-100$) to prevent inflated scores~\cite{merity2016pointer}.
  \item \textbf{Reward score:} Mean output of $r_\phi$ on model-generated responses. Reported separately for SFT and PPO models.
  \item \textbf{Reward gain $\Delta$:} Signed difference $\bar{r}_\text{PPO} - \bar{r}_\text{SFT}$. Positive values indicate improvement.
  \item \textbf{Win rate:} Analytical estimate of the probability that a PPO response scores higher than an SFT response on the same prompt, computed as $\Phi\!\left(\Delta / \sqrt{\sigma_\text{PPO}^2 + \sigma_\text{SFT}^2}\right)$ where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.
  \item \textbf{Diversity (Distinct-1/2):} Fraction of unique unigrams/bigrams in generated text.
  \item \textbf{Overlap (ROUGE-1/2/L~\cite{lin2004rouge}, BLEU-4~\cite{papineni2002bleu}):} N-gram overlap with reference continuations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Hardware}

Experiments were conducted on a dual-GPU workstation with two NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPUs (48\,GB each), 128\,GB RAM, and CUDA 12.1. Pythia-70M experiments ran on GPU~0 and Pythia-160M on GPU~1, enabling full-pipeline training in parallel. SFT required approximately 20--30 minutes per configuration; reward model training required 5--10 minutes; PPO required 45--75 minutes per 500 steps.

\paragraph{Domain sensitivity for Pythia-70M.}
The 70M model benefits measurably from PPO on TinyStories ($\Delta = +0.085$, win rate 52.1\%), the simplest and most structured domain. On CNN/DailyMail and Wikitext-103---where SFT perplexity is 290 and 504 respectively---the same training produces no improvement or a slight regression. This result is consistent with a pre-condition for effective RLHF: the policy must already possess adequate domain fluency for the reward signal to reliably distinguish better from worse outputs. When the base policy is highly uncertain (high PPL), generated text is too noisy for the reward model to provide a stable gradient.

\paragraph{Pythia-160M: near-neutral reward change.}
The 160M model shows reward changes close to zero across all three datasets (absolute $|\Delta| \leq 0.035$ except TinyStories). This is not evidence that PPO failed to train---KL divergence grows steadily and LoRA weights change---but rather that the 160M model's stronger SFT baseline leaves little headroom for improvement within the 500-step budget. The CNN/DailyMail and Wikitext configurations show near-50\% win rates, consistent with no systematic advantage for either checkpoint.

\paragraph{Perplexity-reward trade-off.}
For Pythia-70M, PPO slightly increases perplexity (TinyStories: $98.7\to106.2$; Wikitext: $504.4\to554.2$). This is a well-known trade-off in RLHF: the policy shifts probability mass toward higher-reward outputs that may be less predictable under the original SFT distribution. For Pythia-160M, perplexity is stable or marginally decreases, consistent with the near-zero reward changes.

\paragraph{Reward scale discrepancy.}
Pythia-70M reward models output scores in $[3.8,\,10.9]$ while Pythia-160M reward models output scores in $[-11,\,-6.5]$. This two-order-of-magnitude difference is not meaningful for comparison; it reflects that the reward models (initialized from different SFT checkpoints) converge to different absolute output ranges. Reward scores should be interpreted \emph{within} a model family, not across models.

\subsection{Ablation: Effect of the Merge-and-Reinitialize Strategy}

To quantify the impact of our engineering fix, we compare three PPO configurations for Pythia-70M on TinyStories:

\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt, label=\textbf{(\alph*)}]
  \item \textbf{Naive PEFT}: Pass the SFT PEFT adapter directly to \texttt{PPOTrainer} (frozen LoRA).
  \item \textbf{Manual unfreeze}: Load the PEFT adapter, manually set \texttt{requires\_grad=True} for all LoRA params, use \texttt{ref\_model=None}.
  \item \textbf{Merge-and-reinitialize} (ours): As described in Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}.
\end{enumerate}

Configuration (a) produces zero reward improvement ($\Delta=0.000$, win rate 50.0\%) because no gradient flows through the policy. Configuration (b) trains but causes unbounded ratio explosions due to the missing frozen reference and bfloat16 arithmetic. Only configuration (c) achieves stable training with positive reward gain ($\Delta = +0.085$).

% ─── 6. Analysis ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Analysis}
\label{sec:analysis}

\subsection{Why Does RLHF Help Only on TinyStories for the 70M Model?}

We hypothesize that effective PPO requires two conditions to hold simultaneously: (i) the reward model must be reliable---it must assign clearly higher scores to better text---and (ii) the policy must have sufficient capacity to find and exploit the gradient direction that the reward provides. For Pythia-70M on TinyStories, both conditions are met: SFT perplexity is 98.7 (moderate), the reward model converges well (loss falls from 1.16 to 0.34 within 500 steps), and the domain is simple enough that a 70M model can produce measurably better continuations under RL guidance.

On CNN/DailyMail, SFT perplexity is 290: the model frequently generates repetitive or incoherent text, making the reward model's comparisons noisy. On Wikitext, PPL is 504: the generated text is essentially random for many prompts, so the reward gradient is dominated by noise rather than preference signal.

\subsection{Reward Model Calibration}

The reward score discrepancy between Pythia-70M ($\mu \approx 7$) and Pythia-160M ($\mu \approx -9$) warrants explanation. Both reward models use the same architecture (base model + linear head) and the same training procedure. The difference arises because the 160M reward model is initialized from a much more fluent SFT checkpoint (PPL 18.6 vs.\ 98.7). A more fluent initialization implies that even the \emph{rejected} samples are relatively coherent, producing weaker preference contrasts and driving the scorer toward a regime of large-magnitude negative outputs.

This calibration issue can be addressed in future work via reward normalization (e.g., standardizing output scores to zero mean, unit variance across the training set) or by using a single shared reward model for all model sizes.

% ─── 7. Discussion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Practical implications.}
The merge-and-reinitialize fix (Algorithm~\ref{alg:merge}) is immediately applicable to any project using TRL $\le$0.9.x with LoRA-adapted policies. We recommend it as a standard pre-processing step when combining PEFT and PPO. Similarly, the float32 precision requirement should be treated as a default for models below 200M parameters until future TRL releases provide automatic precision handling.

\paragraph{When should practitioners use RLHF for SLMs?}
Based on our results, we suggest the following heuristic: PPO alignment is likely to be beneficial when the SFT model achieves perplexity below $\approx100$ on the target domain. When perplexity is higher, the practitioner should first invest in better SFT data (e.g., domain-specific corpora, data filtering) before attempting RL alignment.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\begin{enumerate}[topsep=2pt, itemsep=1pt, label=\textbf{L\arabic*.}]
  \item \textbf{Synthetic preference data.} Our preference pairs are constructed automatically rather than annotated by humans. Human-rated pairs would likely produce more reliable reward models and stronger RLHF improvements.
  \item \textbf{Budget constraint.} 500 PPO steps may be insufficient for high-perplexity configurations. Extended training with annealing schedules or adaptive KL penalties could unlock further gains.
  \item \textbf{Reward calibration.} Reward scores are not comparable across model sizes due to differing initialization scales. Future work should normalize rewards to a common scale.
  \item \textbf{Evaluation scope.} We evaluate on text generation quality metrics (reward, PPL, ROUGE) rather than downstream task accuracy or human preference studies. Such evaluations would strengthen the empirical claims.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Promising extensions include: (i) applying DPO~\cite{rafailov2023direct} as an alternative that avoids the RL training loop entirely; (ii) using an external, scale-agnostic reward model (e.g., a 7B model fine-tuned for reward modeling) rather than a model-specific reward model; and (iii) extending experiments to models in the 360M--1B range where RLHF may be more uniformly effective.

% ─── 8. Conclusion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a systematic empirical study of RLHF applied to small language models with 70M and 160M parameters. Our main finding is that RLHF benefits at the SLM scale are \emph{domain-sensitive}: positive reward gains are observed when the SFT model has moderate perplexity on the target domain, while high-perplexity configurations show no clear improvement within a 500-step budget. We identify and fix two critical engineering issues---silent LoRA gradient freeze and bfloat16 ratio explosions---that would otherwise render PPO training ineffective or unstable at this scale. These fixes, formalized in the merge-and-reinitialize strategy, are directly applicable to practitioners using TRL with PEFT. All artifacts are publicly released to lower the barrier to alignment research at accessible compute budgets.
